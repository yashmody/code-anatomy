Why This Matters

DAM folder taxonomy is an architecture decision, not a filing preference. The folder structure chosen in Discovery drives rendition paths, IAM permission groups, CDN cache-key patterns, and AEM replication rules. A brand-first hierarchy (/brand/campaign/locale) and a locale-first hierarchy (/locale/brand/campaign) produce entirely different CDN invalidation scopes and different asset-sharing rules across markets. Getting this wrong in week 2 costs weeks to unwind in Sprint 8 — after assets are referenced in hundreds of content fragments and replication agents are already configured.

Common Pitfall

Teams that skip approval workflow design before CMS configuration consistently ship pages that have never passed legal review. The approval topology — who approves, in what order, with what SLA, with what escalation path — must be modelled as a swimlane diagram before the first workflow is configured in the CMS. Retrofitting approval gates after a compliance incident means touching live workflow definitions, retraining authors mid-project, and auditing every piece of content published in the gap. Design the topology first; wire it second.

Agency Tip

DEPT® field pattern: use a single Content Ready flag in Workfront or Jira that gates both legal sign-off and DAM final-rendition approval simultaneously. Two-track sign-off — legal marks approved while the DAM team is still processing final renditions, or vice versa — is the most common cause of last-minute go-live holds. One flag, two conditions, one gate. Make the flag unset-able only by a named release coordinator, not by either track independently. This eliminates the "legal said yes but the image isn't in the DAM yet" class of incident entirely.

C · Localization, Sitemaps, Approvals, DAM & Velocity

Content is everything the platform manages, displays, stores or publishes. The architect's leverage here isn't in writing copy — it's in the model and the workflow. Get the structure right and every release flows; get it wrong and every release becomes a fire-fight. Six discipline areas decide whether a content programme scales.

1 · Localization workflows

Locale isn't language. en-IN and en-US share a language but differ in spelling, currency, regulation and cultural reference. Plan locales as markets, not as translations.

Authorsource locale→ MT first passDeepL · GPT→ Human translator→ In-country review→ Publish
  • Translation memory — reuse of previously translated strings cuts cost and keeps tone consistent across releases.
  • In-country review — a local team checks the translation in real context, not as a spreadsheet of strings.
  • Tools — AEM multi-site manager, Smartling, Lokalise, Phrase. AI-MT is now strong enough for first pass; humans still needed for tone, idiom and regulated content.

2 · Sitemap

"Sitemap" means two different things — and the architect cares about both:

For humans
IA sitemap

The conceptual site structure — page types, hierarchy, navigation. Drives the content model: what types exist, what attributes each carries.

For search
XML sitemap

The file submitted to search engines for crawling. Auto-generated from the live site; updated as pages are added or retired.

The IA sitemap should live next to the design system, not in a stale slide deck. Without it, authors invent structure ad-hoc and the model fractures within two quarters.

4 · Content approval workflow

Three roles for every piece of content — and they are rarely the same person: creator → approver → publisher. Real-world workflows add stages (editorial, legal, brand, final) and gates.

Draft→ Editorial→ Legal→ Brand→ Publish
  • Named owners at every stage — not "the legal team." A person.
  • SLA on each gate — 24h, 48h, 5 days. Anything unbounded becomes the bottleneck.
  • Audit trail — who approved what, when, why. Compliance gold; also useful when production blames editorial.
  • Escalation — when stuck for more than the SLA, the named escalator decides.
  • Tooling — AEM workflows · Workfront · Jira approval transitions · custom gates.

5 · Asset management — the DAM disciplines

Assets without governance become legal risk, brand drift, and search-by-vibe. Five disciplines keep a DAM honest:

5a · Structure
DAM structure

Folder hierarchy that mirrors business reality — by brand, by campaign, by media type, by locale. One source of truth; no parallel assets-final-v3 shadow folders.

5b · Vocabulary
Tags & taxonomy

A controlled, hierarchical vocabulary (Vehicle > SUV > Compact). Free tags supplement findability, never replace structure. Periodic audits to dedupe synonyms and retire dead tags.

5c · Required
Metadata management

Required fields enforced at upload — no upload without alt-text, usage rights, expiry, photographer, model release. Standard schemas: IPTC, XMP, Dublin Core.

5d · Lifecycle
Asset expiry

Time-bound licences, model and property release dates, brand-mark windows. Auto-alert N days before expiry; workflow to renew or retire. Orphaned expired assets are legal risk.

5e · Acceleration
AI for assets

Auto-tagging by image recognition · auto alt-text and captions · text-to-image first drafts · variant generation (crops, sizes, formats) · semantic search ("confident young professionals in an office").

6 · Content velocity

How quickly content moves from idea to published. The single most underrated metric in content programmes — and the one that decides whether the business ships campaigns weekly or quarterly.

  • Measure — cycle time per content type, throughput per week, time-in-stage per approval gate.
  • Common bottlenecks — approval gates without SLAs · translation queues · design rework loops · waiting on assets that don't exist yet.
  • Levers — templated component-based authoring · smaller faster approval loops · decoupled parallel authoring · automated translation first pass · AI-assisted drafting.
High content velocity is a competitive advantage. The brand that can ship a relevant campaign on a Friday afternoon will out-execute the brand that needs three weeks of approvals — regardless of which has the prettier creative.
Architect's Review · Content
  • Is there a defined content model, and does every type fit it?
  • Is the IA sitemap maintained alongside the design system?
  • Is the source documented for every content type — including who provides and how?
  • Is the approval workflow named (people, SLAs, escalation) — not just understood?
  • Are tags and metadata enforced at upload, or applied retroactively?
  • Are asset expiries tracked, and what happens at expiry?
  • What is the current content velocity per type, and where's the bottleneck?
  • Where is AI assisting today, and where will it next?

Why This Matters

Campaign management requires an architect at the table. Campaign dates drive infrastructure scaling decisions — CDN pre-warming schedules, cache TTL reduction windows, autoscale thresholds, and load test timing are all campaign inputs. A PM who books a campaign launch without the architect knowing means the infrastructure isn't ready for the traffic spike that campaign will generate.

Agency Tip

DEPT® field pattern — the "T-minus board": a shared Confluence page updated daily in the final two weeks, showing every open item by owner and date. Reduces status-meeting time by 60% because status is always current and visible. Every outstanding item has one owner; nothing is "team" or "TBD" on a T-minus board.

Common Pitfall

The most common go-live failure is DNS TTL not reduced 48 hours in advance. A TTL of 3600 on launch day means half your users see the old site for an hour after cut-over. Reduce TTL to 300 two days before the switch; restore it after cut-over is confirmed and stable.

O · PM, Campaigns, Stakeholders, Go-Live, SEO & GEO

The O of CODE is where strategy becomes operational reality. It's the cadence of work — how a campaign moves from idea to live, who signs off, what tools support each step. PMs and Delivery Leads own this layer; architects need to understand it well enough to know where engineering plugs into it.

1 · Project management

Four disciplines hold a project together. They are mundane, repetitive, and the single biggest predictor of whether the team will finish — separate from anything about engineering quality.

Sync
DSM · Daily Stand-up

15-minute daily ritual. Each person answers three questions — what I did yesterday, what I'll do today, what's blocking me. Purpose is surfacing blockers fast, not status reporting.

Tooling
Workflow management

Where work lives across its lifecycle — backlog, in progress, review, blocked, done. Jira (mature, enterprise) and ProofHub (lighter, agency-friendly) are the common picks. The tool isn't the discipline; the workflow definition is.

Authority
Governance

Decision rights (who can approve what), escalation paths (when stuck, who breaks the tie), change control (how scope changes get reviewed), and status reporting cadence.

Ownership
RACI

Responsible does the work · Accountable owns the outcome (one person only, always) · Consulted gives input before · Informed is told after. Fill it in for every key activity; hang the matrix where everyone can see.

2 · Campaign management — planning to measure

Every campaign flows through the same five phases. The work products differ by industry; the phases don't.

Plan→ Build→ Launch→ Measure→ Iterate
  • Plan — brief, audience, objectives, KPIs, budget, timeline, success criteria. If you can't write these on one page, you don't have a campaign yet.
  • Build — creative production, content, landing pages, tech setup, QA, dry-run. The phase with the most opinions per square metre.
  • Launch — go-live checklist, comms to stakeholders, monitoring setup. The point of no return.
  • Measure — daily checks in week one, weekly thereafter; attribution and contribution analysis.
  • Iterate — optimise the live campaign (A/B tests, audience refinement) and document learnings before the next plan starts.

3 · Stakeholder expectation management

The most undervalued discipline in delivery. A technically perfect project with mismanaged expectations is a failed project. A technically average project with managed expectations is a success.

PRINCIPLES

  • Surface assumptions early — what stakeholders think they're getting vs what they're getting. The gap closes only when named out loud.
  • Define scope explicitly — in, out, stretch — and write it down. Verbal scope evaporates by week three.
  • Set the cadence — weekly status, escalation criteria, who hears what when. Predictable rhythm beats brilliant ad-hoc updates.
  • The triangle — time, cost, quality — pick two; the third gives. Make stakeholders make the choice.
  • Write decisions down — hallway conversations evaporate; written decisions hold the team accountable to themselves.
  • Show progress, not just problems — bring two options to status meetings, not one complaint.

RISK PATTERNS TO WATCH

  • Scope creep — every "small addition" compounds. The fourth small addition is the one that breaks the schedule.
  • Late stakeholders — people who arrive late always have opinions. Find them early; you don't get to ignore them.
  • Politics — power dynamics shape what gets prioritised. Name them privately, navigate them deliberately.
  • Silent disagreements — what isn't said in the meeting shows up on the deploy weekend.

5 · SEO & GEO readiness and expectation setting

Two adjacent disciplines, one mature, one brand-new. Both are easy to over-promise on and hard to deliver on.

SEO · Search Engine Optimization

The established discipline — rankings on Google, Bing, etc. Three pillars:

  • Technical SEO — sitemaps, structured data, performance, mobile, crawlability.
  • Content SEO — keyword strategy, topical authority, depth.
  • Off-page SEO — backlinks, mentions, domain authority.
GEO · Generative Engine Optimization

The new discipline — optimising for AI answer engines (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Claude, Google AI Overviews). Focus shifts:

  • From "rank" to "be cited" — appear in the AI's answer, with a source link back.
  • Levers — structured content with clear claims, source links, freshness signals, schema markup, brand mentions across the web.
  • Maturity — no playbook is fully proven yet. Frame as experiments, not commitments.

EXPECTATION SETTING — THE NON-NEGOTIABLE

  • SEO results take 3–6 months minimum to materialise — set this up front, in writing, signed off.
  • GEO is too new for confident promises — frame work as experiments, measure citation rate and inclusion in AI answers, share learning as it accrues.
  • Define success before work starts — rankings? organic traffic? conversions? citations? Pick the metric, pick the target, pick the timeline.
  • Educate on what's controllable — technical hygiene and content are; algorithm changes and competitor moves aren't.

The data layer schema is an architecture contract. When field names change without versioning, CJA dashboards break, AJO journey entry conditions stop evaluating, and Campaign segments return zero results. D · Analytics, Personalization & CDP 101

The D of CODE has two halves — Design (behaviour) and Data (memory). Design we covered above; data is what makes everything else work — analytics, personalization, AI. This deep-dive walks an architect or PM through the data stack in plain language, ending with how an organisation matures from "we have analytics" to "we have a unified customer profile firing real-time decisions across every channel."

What is analytics?

Analytics is the platform's memory of user behaviour. Every page view, click, video play, add-to-cart, abandoned form — it all gets recorded. Without analytics, you guess at what's working. With it, you can answer "how many people did X in the last week, broken down by Y, compared to last month?"

That answer is what every weekly business review, every campaign decision, every product change argument should ultimately be grounded in.

GA4 vs Adobe Analytics

Two dominant platforms. Same purpose, very different ergonomics.

GA4 · Google Analytics 4

Google's analytics platform. Free tier for most teams, paid (GA360) at enterprise scale. Event-based model — everything is an event. Tight integration with the Google ad ecosystem (Ads, Search Console, BigQuery).

Best when · you're SMB to mid-market, already in Google's stack, and want fast time-to-insight without licence cost.

Adobe Analytics

Enterprise-grade, paid. Deep segmentation, custom calculated metrics, flexible report builder. Integrates tightly with the rest of Adobe Experience Cloud (Target, AEM, AEP, AJO). Steeper learning curve.

Best when · enterprise scale, multiple brands or channels, dedicated analytics team, already on Adobe.

A data layer, made concrete

The data layer is the single most underrated piece of any analytics stack. It's a plain JavaScript object that sits on every page, holding the context analytics tools need. Get the data layer right and every tool — GA4, Adobe Analytics, Target, every tag manager — reads from one place. Get it wrong and every team writes their own scraping logic against the DOM.

layout.js — runs on every page
// The data layer is set once, per page, before any tags fire.
window.dataLayer = {
  page: {
    name:     "Product Detail",
    category: "Electronics",
    language: "en-IN"
  },
  product: {
    sku:      "ABC123",
    name:     "Wireless Headphones",
    price:    4999,
    currency: "INR"
  },
  user: {
    type:        "registered",
    loyaltyTier: "gold"
  }
};

// When something happens, push an event.
window.dataLayer.push({
  event:   "product:view",
  product: { sku: "ABC123" }
});

Personalization — Adobe Target

Analytics tells you what happened. Personalization decides what should happen next. Adobe Target is the engine that picks which experience to show which user — and measures whether it worked.

The flow is straightforward:

User arrives→ Which segment?→ Pick experience→ Measure result

Segmentation — how Target groups people

A segment is a rule that defines a group. Rules can be simple ("new visitors") or composite ("mobile users from India with order history greater than three, viewed a product in the last 7 days"). Target evaluates each visitor against your segment rules and slots them into the matching group.

How Target chooses what to show:

  • Rule-based experience — "if user is in segment X, show variation Y." Deterministic, easy to reason about.
  • A/B test — split traffic evenly, measure which variation performs better, ship the winner.
  • Multivariate test — try multiple variations at once, learn which combinations work.
  • Auto-allocate — ML quietly sends more traffic to the winning variation as data accumulates.
  • Auto-target — ML picks the best variation per individual user based on what similar users converted on.

A concrete example: a retail homepage shows "New here? 10% off your first order" to first-time visitors, "Free shipping on orders over ₹2,000" to returning customers, and "VIP early access" to gold-tier loyalty members. Three experiences, three segments, one page — all decided by Target in the first 80ms before render.

Adobe Analytics vs CJA

Adobe Analytics is the classic. Customer Journey Analytics (CJA) is what Adobe built once they accepted that people don't live on one channel any more.

Adobe Analytics · AA

Hit-based and page-view centric. Built around eVars, props and events. Each visit lives in relative isolation; cross-channel joins are limited.

Strength · deep web and app analytics, mature reporting, well-known by analysts.

CJA · Customer Journey Analytics

Sits on top of Adobe Experience Platform (AEP). Journey-based and person-centric. Uses the XDM (Experience Data Model) schema. Can join web, app, call centre, in-store, email and anything else you can ingest.

Strength · cross-channel journeys, one record per real person across every touchpoint.

The difference becomes obvious with one example:

  • AA · "100,000 sessions visited the product page this week."
  • CJA · "Maria viewed the product on web Monday, opened the app Tuesday, called support Wednesday, then purchased on web Thursday — and 8,432 customers showed similar patterns."

The CDP — Customer Data Platform

Every system in your stack has its own opinion of the customer. CRM sees orders. Analytics sees clicks. Support sees tickets. Loyalty sees points. A CDP unifies them all into one profile per real person — and makes that profile actionable in real time.

What a CDP does:

In
Ingest

Pulls data from every system — web, app, CRM, transactions, support, loyalty, email.

Resolve
Identity stitching

Figures out that user@gmail.com on web is the same person as loyalty card #12345 in-store.

Profile
Unify

Builds one record per real person — attributes, behaviour, computed traits, segment memberships.

Out
Activate

Pushes segments and profile data to downstream systems — Target, Marketo, AJO, ad platforms — in milliseconds.

Common products · Adobe Real-Time CDP (RT-CDP) · Segment (Twilio) · Tealium AudienceStream · mParticle · Salesforce CDP · Treasure Data

Side note · Data lakes

Many large organisations also have a data lake — Snowflake, Databricks, BigQuery, an S3 bucket. It holds the raw firehose from everywhere: clicks, transactions, logs, IoT, ad data, third-party feeds. Data scientists query it for analysis; ML models train on it; BI tools build dashboards from it.

A CDP and a data lake are not the same thing — they coexist:

  • CDP — real-time, identity-resolved, profile-centric. Built for activation (sending segments to downstream marketing tools in milliseconds).
  • Data lake — batch or stream, raw, schema-on-read. Built for analysis and ML (data science notebooks, BI dashboards, model training).

The data lake often feeds the CDP with historical context; the CDP often emits real-time events back to the lake for long-term storage. They are complementary, not redundant.

The Generic Event Dispatcher — in depth

The common description is "build one central dispatcher." But how do two genuinely unrelated scripts — in different components, different files, possibly loaded at different times — share that one dispatcher without importing each other?

The answer is a stable, semantically appropriate HTML element with a known identity. Give it a fixed id that is guaranteed to exist on every page. That element becomes the shared wire. Any script that knows the selector can find it and either fire events on it or listen to events on it. The two parties share nothing except that selector — and the element type should make it obvious this wire is not part of the UI.

Without the bus

Component A must hold a direct reference to Component B, or walk up the DOM hoping a shared parent catches the bubble. Sender and receiver are coupled — change one, break the other.

With the bus element

Component A and Component B both know only one thing: #event-bus. Neither knows the other exists. Add or remove listeners with zero changes to the sender.

Choosing the right anchor element

The first instinct is to reach for a <div>. Don't. A <div> is a block container for visual content — using it as an invisible event wire is semantically misleading to anyone reading the markup, and it offers nothing that makes the intent clear. Two better options exist:

Option 1 · <template>

A native HTML5 element that is explicitly never rendered. Its entire purpose is to hold inert content. No box, no paint, no layout impact — and the element itself sits in the DOM ready to receive event listeners. The semantics announce the intent: this is structural, not visual.

Option 2 · Custom element

An undeclared tag like <event-bus> is treated by browsers as a plain HTMLElement — inline, unstyled, zero visual footprint. It's self-documenting: anyone reading the HTML immediately understands what this element is for, without needing a comment.

<template> is the safer default — it's a standard element with a spec-defined "I am not for display" contract. The custom element is better when you want the markup itself to read like documentation. Either way, never a <div>.

Step 1 — place the anchor element

One element, in the shared layout or shell — guaranteed to exist on every page before any component script runs.

Recommended — <template> (layout.html)
<!-- <template> is never rendered. It has no box, no paint,
     no layout impact — its only job is to exist in the DOM. -->
<template id="event-bus"></template>
Alternative — custom element (layout.html)
<!-- Unknown tags → plain HTMLElement. No rendering, no styles.
     The tag name is the comment. -->
<event-bus id="app"></event-bus>

Step 2 — Party A dispatches

The sending component knows nothing about who is listening. It creates a CustomEvent with a namespaced type and a detail payload, then fires it on the bus element. Notice bubbles: false — the event does not travel up the DOM tree at all; it fires exactly on the element you choose and stops there.

search-filter.js — unaware of results-panel.js
// Find the shared bus on the current page.
const bus = document.querySelector('#event-bus');

// Build a typed event with a payload.
const event = new CustomEvent('filter:changed', {
  detail:  { query: 'adobe AEM', facets: ['docs'] },
  bubbles: false,   // ← stays on this element; no propagation
  composed: false   // ← does not cross shadow-DOM boundaries
});

bus.dispatchEvent(event);

Step 3 — Party B listens

The receiving component knows nothing about who dispatched. It finds the same element and adds a listener for the type it cares about. Even if Party A hasn't loaded yet, the listener just waits — no race condition because the bus element is always in the DOM.

results-panel.js — unaware of search-filter.js
// Same selector — this is the only thing the two parties share.
const bus = document.querySelector('#event-bus');

bus.addEventListener('filter:changed', (e) => {
  // e.detail contains whatever Party A put in.
  renderResults(e.detail.query, e.detail.facets);
});

Why this bypasses the propagation flow

Normal DOM events bubble upward — a click on a button travels button → section → main → body → document → window. Both sender and receiver need to be in the same ancestry, and you risk accidentally catching unrelated events that bubble through the same path.

The bus element collapses that entirely. The event is dispatched to a specific element with bubbles: false. It fires, listeners on that element receive it, and it stops. DOM position of either party is irrelevant — a sidebar component and a modal can communicate even though they share no common parent in the DOM tree.

Namespace your event types with a category:action pattern — cart:add, user:login, filter:changed, page:ready. This prevents two unrelated modules from colliding on a generic name like change or update.

Module 05 · New

State — what the system remembers between moments

Every modern application reduces to one equation. It fits on a napkin:

Current State + Event = New State

That equation is why React, Redux, MobX, Zustand, Angular Signals — every modern frontend framework — exists. They are all elaborations of three words: state plus event. Get the equation right and every framework becomes legible at a glance.

What state actually is

State is everything the system has to remember between moments. Variables hold individual values; state is the aggregate that defines what the user is currently looking at, who they are, where they are in a flow, and what the system is doing on their behalf.

  • Authentication state — who is logged in and what they can see.
  • Application state — the overall mode the app is in (loading, ready, error, offline).
  • Workflow state — where the user is in a multi-step process.
  • Page state — what's on screen right now, what's selected, what's open.
  • Domain state — cart contents, search results, the current document.

The single source of truth

State must live in one place. Render reads from it. Biz writes to it. API hydrates it. The moment two places hold the "same" piece of state, they drift — and the rest of the project is spent chasing why one screen shows old data.

If you can't answer "where does this value live?" in one breath, your state is fragmented.

This is why Stalwart (Module 09 onwards) treats state.js as a first-class file — distinct from model.js. The model owns the shape of an entity; state owns the current value of that entity in the running app.

Agency Tip

Common Pitfall

Sorting on the client after a server-side paginated response. The sort applies to the current page only; users see inconsistent ordering as they paginate. Contracts — what architects actually talk about

An implementation can change tomorrow. A contract is the promise that must hold. Once you start thinking in contracts you stop arguing about how code is written and start arguing about what it promises to do — which is a much shorter and more useful conversation.

The four contracts in any system

External
API Contract

Endpoints, methods, payload shapes, error codes, rate limits, versioning. The promise to the world outside your codebase.

Internal
Event Contract

The event name (domain:action), its payload shape, who produces it, who may consume it. The promise between modules.

Shape

The schema of an entity — mandatory and optional fields, validation rules, allowed transitions. The promise about the data itself.

Boundary
Component Contract

Props in, events out, slots/children. The public surface of a reusable building block.

Why contracts are the bridge to AI-generated code

This is the most important paragraph of Part I. Contracts are what you hand to AI; implementations are what AI hands back. The architect owns the contracts; the AI fills in the implementations. Get the contracts right and the implementation is mostly mechanical. Get them wrong and no amount of clever prompting will fix it.

This is also why the Stalwart prompt (inside CODER's C) begins by stating the file contract, the event contract, and the data flow — before naming a single feature. The architect's leverage is here.

An architect who controls the contracts controls the architecture. An architect who only reviews code reviews symptoms.

The D of CODER isn't just CI/CD. It's the entire layer that turns your code into a service people can reach. The mental model

Every production system is built by stacking components. Each new component solves a problem that emerged when the previous one wasn't enough. Walk it from the bottom up and the whole picture becomes legible.

Layer 1 · The web server

Step 1 — the simplest deployment

The web server answers HTTP requests. It listens on a port (80 for HTTP, 443 for HTTPS), reads incoming requests and returns responses — static files directly, dynamic requests by proxying to an app server. The two dominant choices are Nginx (event-driven, fast at high concurrency) and Apache (older, plugin-rich, fork-per-request).

A single web server can host multiple sites by routing on the incoming Host header. That's a virtual host (vhost) — and it's how one box serves www.brand-a.com and www.brand-b.com without confusion.

  server_name www.example.com;

  index index.html;

  location /api/ {

Computers talk in IP addresses. Humans talk in names. DNS is the lookup that translates www.example.com into 203.0.113.42. The browser asks DNS first, then connects to whatever IP came back.

The records to know: A (name → IPv4), AAAA (name → IPv6), CNAME (name → another name), MX (mail), TXT (verification, SPF, DKIM). Every record has a TTL — how long resolvers may cache the answer. Lower TTL = faster failover, more lookup load.

Layer 3 · SSL / TLS

HTTPS is HTTP wrapped in TLS — the cryptographic handshake that proves the server is who it claims and encrypts the traffic. Three things to know:

  • Certificates prove identity. Free from Let's Encrypt (90-day rotation, automated). Commercial CAs sell extended-validation certs that still show the company name in some contexts.
  • Termination — where TLS gets decrypted. Often at the load balancer or CDN, so internal traffic is plain HTTP inside a trusted network.
  • Modern hygiene — TLS 1.3, strong cipher suites, HSTS header to force HTTPS, and certificate auto-renewal.

VMs in brief. EC2 on AWS and Azure VMs on Azure are virtual machines running on someone else's hardware. You pick the size, OS, region; they boot in minutes; you pay by the second. The bigger trend, though, is away from raw VMs toward managed services — databases, queues, containers — where you don't manage the OS at all.

Service models · IaaS · PaaS · SaaS · FaaS

Cloud isn't one thing. It's a ladder of responsibility models, each handing more of the operations stack to the provider in exchange for less control. Architects pick a level per workload — sometimes per service. Getting this wrong costs you either money (paying for managed services you don't need) or velocity (running your own OS for problems someone else solved).

Who owns what · the responsibility ladder
On-premyou own everything
IaaSyou own OS upward
PaaSyou own code + data
FaaSyou own a function
SaaSyou own configuration

IaaS · Infrastructure as a Service

You get a virtual machine. The provider runs the hardware, hypervisor and network; you run the OS, the runtime, the application. Maximum control, maximum operational burden.

  • Examples · AWS EC2 · Azure VMs · GCP Compute Engine · DigitalOcean Droplets
  • You own · OS patching, runtime versions, security groups, capacity planning, scaling, monitoring agents
  • Right when · you need a specific kernel, specific GPU, custom networking, or you're lifting-and-shifting from on-prem
  • Wrong when · the workload is a standard web app — you're paying yourself to do work a PaaS would do for you

PaaS · Platform as a Service

You hand the provider your code; they run it. The OS, runtime, autoscaling, deployment pipeline, load balancing are all handled. You configure; you don't operate.

  • Examples · AWS Elastic Beanstalk · Azure App Service · Google App Engine · Heroku · Vercel · Netlify · Adobe AEM as a Cloud Service (AEMaaCS)
  • You own · your code, the data inside the platform's databases, the configuration, the budget
  • Right when · standard stack (Node, Python, Java, .NET), standard scaling profile, you want to optimise for velocity over control
  • Wrong when · you need OS-level customisation, you're cost-sensitive at very high scale (PaaS economics break above a threshold), or vendor lock-in is a deal-breaker

FaaS · Functions as a Service (serverless)

The unit of deployment isn't an app — it's a single function. The provider runs it only when triggered, scales it from zero to thousands of concurrent executions, and bills per invocation and per millisecond of compute. The most extreme abstraction in the ladder.

  • Examples · AWS Lambda · Azure Functions · GCP Cloud Functions · Cloudflare Workers · Vercel Functions
  • You own · the function code, its dependencies, the event source binding
  • Right when · event-driven workloads (webhooks, queue consumers, scheduled jobs), spiky traffic that goes to zero, glue code between services
  • Wrong when · long-running compute, stateful workloads, predictable steady load (cheaper as a container), latency-sensitive paths (cold-start tax)
  • The cold-start trap · first invocation after idle can take 100ms–2s depending on runtime. Manageable with provisioned concurrency — but provisioned concurrency is just paying for an always-on server you said you didn't want

SaaS · Software as a Service

You don't run anything. You log in, configure, and pay. The product itself is the service.

  • Examples · Salesforce · HubSpot · Workday · Stripe · SendGrid · Auth0 · Datadog · the consumer end of Google Workspace, Microsoft 365
  • You own · your data inside the product, your account configuration, your integration glue
  • Right when · the function is non-differentiating for your business (CRM, email, observability, auth). Build differentiates; buy enables.
  • Wrong when · the function is your business (your CRM IS your product → build it). Or compliance prevents data leaving your perimeter.

The honourable mentions · BaaS, DBaaS, containers

  • BaaS · Backend as a Service — Firebase, Supabase, AWS Amplify. Auth + database + storage + functions, pre-wired. Excellent for prototypes and mobile-first products; lock-in risk grows with scale.
  • DBaaS · Database as a Service — RDS, Cloud SQL, MongoDB Atlas, Redis Cloud, Neon. Managed databases as PaaS-flavoured services. The default for any new project; running your own database in 2026 needs a reason.
  • Containers · between IaaS and PaaS — ECS, EKS, GKE, AKS, Cloud Run, Fly.io. You bring an image; they run it. More portable than PaaS (Docker image is the contract), more operational than serverless. The most common modern default.

Choosing the right level

Three questions decide the level:

  • 1. Is this workload your differentiator? If yes, you want control — IaaS or containers. If no, you want speed — PaaS or SaaS.
  • 2. What's the traffic shape? Spiky-to-zero → FaaS. Steady → containers or PaaS. Unpredictable peaks → containers with autoscaling.
  • 3. What does exit cost? Higher abstraction → more lock-in. SaaS data export plans should be checked before signing, not when you want to leave.
The right answer is rarely one level. A real production system mixes them: PaaS-hosted API in containers, FaaS for webhook handlers, DBaaS for primary store, SaaS for email/auth/payments, IaaS for the one workload nobody else does. The architect's job is knowing which goes where and being able to defend each choice.

Autoscaling

Traffic isn't flat. Autoscaling adds and removes capacity in response to demand, so you neither over-provision (wasted money) nor under-provision (failed requests).

Vertical

Make the same machine bigger — more CPU, more RAM. Hits a ceiling fast and usually requires a reboot. Useful for databases and stateful systems where horizontal scaling is hard.

Horizontal · default

Add more identical machines behind the load balancer. Scales further, fails more gracefully (one box dies, the rest carry on), works only if the app is stateless or state is externalised.

What triggers a scale event

  • CPU utilization — classic. Scale up when sustained CPU > 70%, scale down when < 30%.
  • Request rate — requests per second across the fleet.
  • Queue depth — for async workers, scale the worker pool to drain the queue.
  • Custom metrics — anything you can publish: latency, error rate, business signals.

Three numbers an architect must set: min (never fewer than X), max (never more than Y — protects the bill), and cooldown (wait N minutes between scale actions, to avoid flapping). The set of identical instances being managed is the scaling group (or autoscaling group on AWS).

How to read them

  • On a single box — tail -f follows new lines as they arrive; grep filters; awk extracts fields.

Caching · the five tiers

Caching is the single largest performance lever in a production system — and the single largest source of subtle correctness bugs. Every production stack caches in five places, each with its own purpose, eviction policy, and failure mode. An architect should be able to point at every tier and say what lives there, how it's invalidated, and what happens when it goes wrong.

The five tiers · from browser back to database
Browser cacheCache-Control · ETag
CDN cacheedge · global
App-layer cacheDispatcher · reverse proxy
Origin cacheRedis · Memcached
Data cacheDB query cache · ORM cache

Tier 1 · Browser cache

The closest cache to the user — the user's own browser. Controlled by HTTP response headers. Free, fast, and the easiest to misconfigure.

  • Cache-Control — the master directive. max-age=N means cache for N seconds. public means any cache may store it; private means only the browser (not the CDN). no-store means never cache. immutable tells the browser "this URL will never change" — the only legitimate use is fingerprinted asset URLs.
  • ETag — a content-derived identifier the server returns. The browser sends it back as If-None-Match on the next request; the server replies 304 Not Modified if unchanged. Saves bandwidth but still costs a round-trip.
  • stale-while-revalidate — serve the stale copy immediately, refresh in the background. Hides revalidation latency from the user. The single most underused directive on the modern web.
  • Vary — declares which request headers change the response. Vary: Accept-Encoding means cache one copy per encoding. Get this wrong and users see other users' content.

Tier 2 · CDN (edge cache)

Akamai, Cloudflare, Fastly, CloudFront — POPs around the world holding cached copies of your responses. A CDN cache hit at the edge means your origin never sees the request. Cache hit ratio is the single number an architect should know off the top of their head; at 90% hit ratio, your origin handles 10% of traffic.

TTL-based

"Cache this for 1 hour." Simple, predictable. Wrong content lives at the edge until the TTL expires — acceptable for marketing pages, dangerous for inventory.

Purge-based

Cache forever; explicitly purge when the source changes. Faster correctness, more operational complexity. Soft purge (mark stale, revalidate on next request) and hard purge (remove immediately) are different things — pick the right one.

  • Surrogate keys / cache tags — tag every cached response with logical identifiers (product-1234, category-shoes). Purge by tag when the underlying entity changes. The only sane way to manage invalidation at scale.
  • Cache-key design — what makes two requests "the same"? Path is the floor; add query parameters, headers, cookies as needed. Get this wrong by including a session cookie in the key and you cache one copy per user — which is no caching at all.
  • Origin shield — a single regional POP that absorbs all origin requests, so a CDN-wide purge doesn't stampede the origin. Fastly's term; most CDNs have an equivalent.

Tier 3 · App-layer cache (Dispatcher / reverse proxy)

Inside your own perimeter, but in front of the app server. In AEM, the Dispatcher is exactly this — a hardened Apache module that caches rendered HTML and serves it without ever touching the Publishers. Outside AEM, the equivalent is Nginx with a proxy_cache or Varnish.

  • What lives here — fully rendered HTML, JSON API responses, static assets that aren't already on the CDN. The "warm middle" of the stack.
  • Statfile invalidation (AEM) — Dispatcher invalidates by touching a statfile; any cached file older than the statfile is treated as stale. Simple but coarse — be deliberate about which paths share a statfile.
  • Bypass conditions — authenticated requests, query strings, specific cookies. If your Dispatcher caches a personalised page, you have a data leak. If it bypasses for every cookie, you have no cache.
  • The cache-miss storm — when the cache flushes and 1,000 concurrent requests all miss and hit the origin together. Mitigate with request coalescing (one origin request, many waiting clients) or staggered TTLs.

Tier 4 · Origin cache (Redis / Memcached)

Inside the application, in front of the database. The application asks "is this in Redis?" before issuing a database query. Used for sessions, computed results, rate-limit counters, anything expensive to compute but cheap to store.

  • Cache-aside — app checks cache; on miss, queries DB and writes the result back. The default pattern.
  • Write-through — every write hits cache and DB together. Consistency at the cost of write latency.
  • Write-behind — writes go to cache immediately, DB asynchronously. Fast writes, weaker durability. Use carefully.
  • Eviction policies — LRU (least recently used) is the safe default. LFU (least frequently used) wins for hot-key workloads. TTL-only loses both ways.
  • The hot key problem — one extremely popular key (a celebrity profile, a flash-sale product) saturates a single Redis node. Solutions: replicate the key, shard differently, or add a small in-process cache in the app layer.

Tier 5 · Data cache (DB query cache, ORM cache)

The cache you didn't know was there — and the one most teams accidentally fight against. Modern databases cache query plans and result buffers; ORMs cache loaded entities within a session. None of this survives a process restart.

  • The "phantom slow query" — fine in dev, fine on the first prod request, then mysteriously fast forever. That's the DB buffer cache warming up. Real perf testing must include cold-cache scenarios.
  • ORM session cache — Hibernate's L1, ActiveRecord's identity map. Helpful within a request, dangerous if leaked across requests.
  • L2 / second-level cache — application-wide, cross-request. Powerful but introduces a third cache layer to invalidate. Usually a sign you should be using Tier 4 (Redis) instead.

Invalidation — the hard problem

"There are only two hard things in computer science: cache invalidation and naming things." The quote exists because cache invalidation is the most common source of weird production bugs an architect will encounter. Three strategies, each with a trade:

TTL
Time-based expiry

Set a duration, walk away. Simple, predictable, eventually wrong. Use when "wrong for N minutes" is acceptable — most marketing content qualifies.

Purge
Event-driven eviction

Cache forever; explicitly invalidate on source change. Correct, complex, easy to miss a code path. Use when freshness matters — inventory, prices, status.

Versioned URLs
Cache the immutable

Change the URL when content changes (/css/app.a1b2c3d.css). Cache for a year. The cleanest pattern there is — but only works for assets you control the URL of.

The failure pattern to watch for: tier-skipping invalidation. You purge the CDN but forget the app-layer cache; you flush Redis but forget the CDN. Stale content lives in whichever tier you forgot. The fix is to invalidate top-down (browser → CDN → Dispatcher → Redis) and to instrument purge calls so you can prove every tier was hit.

If you can't draw the cache topology of your production system on a whiteboard in 60 seconds, you don't know where your bugs are going to come from.

Observability · beyond logs

Logs (covered above) tell you what individual events happened. Observability is the broader discipline: being able to ask a question of your running system that you didn't think to instrument in advance. The three pillars are logs, metrics, and traces — each answers a different kind of question.

Logs
What happened, in order

One line per event. High cardinality, high cost. Best for forensic detail: "why did this specific request fail?"

Metrics
How much, over time

Counters, gauges, histograms. Low cardinality, cheap to store, cheap to query. Best for trends and alerts: "is error rate climbing?"

Traces
How one request flowed

The end-to-end path of a single request across services, with timing at each hop. Best for latency hunts: "where did this request lose 800ms?"

SLIs, SLOs, error budgets

Metrics on their own are noise. What turns them into operational decisions is a service level objective — a target you've committed to, expressed in user-visible terms.

  • SLI · Service Level Indicator. A measurable quantity. "Percentage of homepage requests served in < 800ms."
  • SLO · Service Level Objective. The target for an SLI. "99.5% of homepage requests in < 800ms, measured over 28 days."
  • Error budget · 100% minus the SLO. If your SLO is 99.5%, you have 0.5% of requests you're permitted to break per period. Spending the budget is fine; running out of budget is a deploy freeze.
  • SLA · Service Level Agreement. The contract with the customer — usually looser than your internal SLO, because you want headroom.

The discipline this enforces: perfect is not the target. The team is free to deploy aggressively while there's budget; the team must slow down and harden when there isn't. Without an explicit budget, every incident is a fight about whether to ship the next feature.

RED & USE — the dashboard starter kits

RED · per service

Rate (requests per second) · Errors (% failing) · Duration (latency percentiles, p50/p95/p99). The three numbers every microservice dashboard must show. If you don't know your p99 latency, you don't know your service.

USE · per resource

Utilization · Saturation · Errors. For every resource (CPU, RAM, disk, network), the three numbers that tell you it's healthy. Saturation (work queued waiting for the resource) is the most underused signal.

Distributed tracing

One request crosses ten services; something is slow; which hop is the offender? Distributed tracing is the answer. Every request gets a trace ID; every downstream call gets a span ID; the trace assembles into a flame graph showing exactly where time was spent.

The standard is OpenTelemetry (OTel) — vendor-neutral instrumentation, pluggable backends. Most APM vendors ingest OTel directly now (Datadog, New Relic, Honeycomb, Grafana Tempo, AWS X-Ray).

Synthetic vs real-user monitoring

Lab tests vs field tests, applied to production. Both are needed; each lies in a different direction.

  • Synthetic · scripted probes from known locations on a schedule. Catches outages, regional degradation, certificate expiry. Lies about real-user experience because the probes don't match user device distributions.
  • RUM (Real User Monitoring) · JS in the page reports actual user-experienced metrics. Captures the long tail (old devices, bad networks). Lies about availability because users who can't load the page can't report.
  • Use both. Alert on synthetic for availability; track RUM for experience quality. Lighthouse is a lab tool; CrUX is a field tool. Believe the field tool.

Alerts that don't get ignored

The fastest way to destroy on-call is to alert on every error line. Three rules:

  • Symptom over cause — alert on "users see errors", not "CPU is high". CPU high is a clue, not an outage.
  • Burn-rate alerts — alert when you're burning the error budget faster than the SLO allows. Short-window fast-burn (catch outages) plus long-window slow-burn (catch chronic degradation).
  • Page only what humans must act on now — everything else is a ticket. If 60% of pages are "investigated, no action", you're paging the wrong things.
A system you can't observe is a system you can't operate. The cost of observability is not the cost of dashboards — it is the cost of instrumenting code the day before you need it.

Why This Matters

Contract testing is necessary beyond integration tests. An integration test validates that A and B work together in the test environment today. A contract test validates that B will not change its API in a way that breaks A — even when A and B are tested separately, by different teams, on different schedules. Consumer-driven contracts (Pact) are the correct tool for this. Without contract tests, the integration layer gives you confidence in the present; contract tests give you confidence across time and organisational boundaries.

O · Testing · Page Speed · Accessibility

Three quality gates, three different conversations. Testing asks "does it behave correctly?" Page speed asks "does it behave fast enough?" Accessibility asks "does it behave for everyone?" None of them is optional, and none of them is solved by a tool — each is a discipline of writing code differently from the start.

Testing · the pyramid & what each layer asks

Testing isn't one thing. It's a stack of confidence-building layers, each cheaper than the one above and each catching different bugs. The classical test pyramid says most tests should be unit, some integration, few end-to-end. Teams violate it all the time and pay for it in slow CI and flaky pipelines.

The test pyramid · base is fastest, apex is slowest
E2E · few · slow · brittle
Integration · some · medium speed
Unit · many · fast · stable
Unit
One function, no dependencies

Pure logic, mocked I/O. Runs in milliseconds. Catches: bad math, wrong branches, off-by-one. Doesn't catch: wiring problems.

Integration
Multiple units together

Real database, real cache, real internal calls — external services still mocked. Catches: schema drift, serialisation bugs, transaction issues. Doesn't catch: end-user flows.

End-to-end
The full system, browser to DB

Playwright, Cypress, Selenium. Catches: the things only a real user sees. Costs: slow, flaky, expensive to maintain. Keep them few and focused on critical user journeys.

Test cases — how to structure one

Two patterns. Pick one and apply it everywhere.

AAA · Arrange, Act, Assert

Three sections, each one to a few lines. Arrange sets up state. Act performs the one operation under test. Assert checks the outcome. If you have two "Acts" in one test, it's two tests. Engineering standard.

Given-When-Then

BDD flavour. Given the system in state X, when action Y happens, then outcome Z. Reads as specification — better for tests that double as documentation, business-readable acceptance criteria, contract testing.

The single best discipline is to name tests after the bug they prevent:

test names that work as documentation
// Bad — describes the implementation
test('getUserById returns user', ...)

// Good — describes the behaviour and edge
test('getUserById returns null when user is soft-deleted', ...)
test('getUserById throws when called with empty string', ...)
test('getUserById returns user even if email is uppercased', ...)

Coverage — a floor, never a goal

"100% test coverage" is a meaningless target. Coverage tells you what code ran during the test suite; it doesn't tell you what code is actually verified. You can hit 100% coverage with tests that never assert anything.

  • Set a coverage floor — typically 70–80% line coverage on new code, with branch coverage tracked separately. Fail the build if it drops.
  • Don't chase 100% — the last 10% is testing trivial getters and framework boilerplate. Diminishing returns, increasing brittleness.
  • Mutation testing — if you want to know whether your tests are good, not just present. Stryker (JS), Pitest (Java) inject deliberate bugs and verify a test fails. Game-changing for codebases that already have high coverage and still ship bugs.
  • Coverage on the critical path matters more than overall coverage. Payment flow at 95% beats overall at 85%.

Automation — what to automate, what to leave alone

Three categories of testing work; only one should be automated by default:

Always automate
Regression

Anything that has already broken once. Anything on the critical path. Anything you'd otherwise run by hand every release.

Sometimes automate
Smoke & sanity

Post-deploy "is the site up" checks. Synthetic monitors do this for you in production; in CI, keep them minimal — under 5 minutes total.

Don't automate
Exploratory

"Click around and see what feels wrong." Genuinely valuable, genuinely human. Automating exploratory testing is automating the thing you're trying to do, which is to not follow the script.

AEM-specific testing layers

  • AEM Mocks (io.wcm) — the standard mock framework for Sling, JCR, OSGi. Unit tests for Sling Models, Servlets, OSGi services without spinning up a JVM-AEM instance.
  • HTL unit tests — Sightly Test Framework. Verify that templates render correctly with given Sling Model inputs. Often skipped; should not be.
  • Integration tests — Cloud Manager pipelines run UI tests against a real AEM instance in CM-Test. Selenium / WebDriver / Playwright as the runner.
  • Performance tests — JMeter or Gatling, hitting Dispatcher. Test under realistic concurrency, with realistic cache hit ratios — not just cold cache.
  • Content/migration tests — Groovy scripts on package upload to verify migrated content matches schema. The cheapest way to catch a migration bug before launch.

The CI feedback loop

Tests only catch bugs if developers actually run them. Tests only get run if the loop is fast. Under 10 minutes from push to green is the threshold below which developers trust CI and above which they start ignoring it.

  • Parallelise aggressively — split test suites across runners. Most CI providers charge per minute, not per runner.
  • Order by speed — unit first, integration next, e2e last. Fail fast on the cheap layers.
  • Quarantine flakes immediately — a flaky test is worse than no test. It teaches the team to ignore failures.
  • Cache the slow bits — dependency installs, container images, build artefacts. Re-downloading node_modules on every run is engineering self-harm.
A test suite isn't "done" when it's written. It's done when it has been trusted by the team for six months. The decisions that lead there happen on day one.

Page Speed · the score that's not the goal

Page speed is the user-visible side of CODER's O letter. Caching (in Deployment 101 above) is the infrastructure-side answer; this is the application-side answer. They reinforce each other — neither alone is enough.

Core Web Vitals — what Google measures, what users feel

Three numbers, each tied to a phase of the user's experience:

LCP
Largest Contentful Paint

How long until the biggest visible element renders. Hero image or H1, usually. Good: < 2.5s. The classic "page feels slow" metric.

INP
Interaction to Next Paint

How long after a click/tap does the page respond visually. Replaced FID in 2024. Good: < 200ms. Reveals JS-heavy event handlers.

CLS
Cumulative Layout Shift

How much the page jumps around as it loads. Banners, late-loading ads, web fonts. Good: < 0.1. The most fixable, most ignored vital.

Lab vs field — Lighthouse lies, CrUX tells

Lab (Lighthouse)

Single run, controlled conditions, throttled to a slow phone. Useful for catching regressions in CI. Does not reflect what real users experience — your users aren't all on a Moto G4.

Field (CrUX / RUM)

Real measurements from real browsers in the wild, aggregated by Google's CrUX dataset or your own RUM. This is what Google uses for ranking. Believe this number.

The critical rendering path

The browser needs HTML, CSS, and (sometimes) JS before it can paint. Every blocking resource extends time-to-first-paint. The optimisation is conceptually simple: defer everything that isn't on the critical path.

  • HTML first — server-rendered HTML lets the browser start parsing immediately. SPAs that render entirely in JS lose this.
  • Critical CSS inlined — the CSS needed for above-the-fold rendering goes in a <style> tag in <head>. Everything else is async.
  • JS deferred — defer or async on every script. Scripts in <head> without one of these are render-blocking.
  • Third-party tags last — analytics, chat, A/B, tag managers. Measure their cost in milliseconds; you'll be horrified.

Images — the heaviest payload, the biggest win

Images are usually 50–70% of page weight. They're also where the biggest wins live, and the wins are mostly mechanical.

  • Format · AVIF > WebP > JPEG > PNG. AVIF saves another 30% over WebP. Use <picture> with format fallbacks.
  • Responsive · srcset + sizes. Mobile gets a smaller file; desktop gets the right resolution. AEM image renditions automate this; EDS does it natively.
  • Lazy-load · loading="lazy" on every below-the-fold image. Don't lazy-load the LCP image — that's a common own goal.
  • Width/height attributes · always. The browser reserves the space before the image downloads, eliminating CLS from images.

JavaScript — the budget

The single best tool an engineering lead has is a JS budget per route. Above the budget, the build fails. The number depends on the audience; common starting points: 170 KB compressed for marketing sites, 250 KB for app-heavy interfaces.

  • Code splitting — load only what's needed for the current route. Webpack, Vite, esbuild all do this. Default for Next.js and modern frameworks.
  • Tree shaking — drop unused exports at build time. Watch out for libraries that ship as side-effectful modules; they kill tree shaking.
  • Audit dependencies — every npm install is a perf decision. Bundlephobia exists. Use it.
  • The third-party tag problem — that one tag manager adds 800ms to LCP. Move tags server-side where possible (Edge tag managers, server-side GTM).

Fonts — the silent CLS culprit

  • Preload critical fonts — <link rel="preload" as="font" crossorigin>.
  • font-display: swap — show a fallback while the custom font loads. Users see text immediately.
  • Subset — ship only the glyphs you use. A 200 KB font becomes 30 KB. Variable fonts (one file, many weights) often win overall.
  • System font stack for body copy is a legitimate choice — zero font payload, zero CLS, native feel.

AEM-specific levers

  • Dispatcher cache rules — covered in Deployment 101. The biggest LCP lever on AEM is hitting Dispatcher cache, not Publisher.
  • EDS (Edge Delivery Services) — Adobe's decoupled-authoring path. Eliminates the heavy HTL render entirely. CLS and LCP scores typically jump 30+ points moving from classic AEM to EDS for the same content.
  • Image renditions — pre-generate srcset variants at upload time. Dispatcher serves them directly.
  • Client libs (clientlibs) — concatenate, minify, version. Mind the order: don't load author-only libs on publish.
  • Lazy authoring — components below the fold render on demand, not on page request.
Page speed isn't a phase. It's a budget. Every commit either spends from it or earns it back. Teams that publish their budgets on a wall hit them; teams that don't, don't.

Accessibility · WCAG as the agency floor

The third quality gate. Often the one teams retrofit at the end, which is the most expensive way to do it. Accessibility designed in costs almost nothing; accessibility retrofitted into a launched system costs a redesign.

WCAG — what to target

WCAG 2.2 Level AA is the agency floor. AAA is for specialist contexts (government, regulated industries with accessibility mandates). AA covers what 95% of users actually need.

WCAG organises around four principles (POUR):

  • Perceivable — text alternatives for images, captions for video, sufficient contrast, content that works when the page is zoomed 200%.
  • Operable — every interaction works from the keyboard, no traps, focus visible at all times, skip-links to bypass nav.
  • Understandable — language declared, error messages clear, forms labelled, behaviour predictable.
  • Robust — semantic HTML, valid markup, ARIA only where native semantics aren't enough.

The seven 80% wins

Most accessibility complaints come from a small set of fixable things. Get these right and most users stop being blocked:

  • Semantic HTML — <button> not <div onclick>. <nav>, <main>, <article>. Free behaviour, free accessibility.
  • Alt text on every image — descriptive, not decorative ("CFO Maria Hernandez at quarterly call", not "image1.jpg"). Decorative images get alt="", not omitted alt.
  • Form labels — every input has a <label>, programmatically associated. Placeholder text is not a label.
  • Keyboard reachability — tab through the page. Every interactive element reachable, in a logical order, with visible focus state.
  • Colour contrast — 4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large text. Free tools will catch this in CI.
  • Heading hierarchy — one <h1> per page. <h2> children, <h3> grandchildren. Screen reader users navigate by headings.
  • Focus management — when a modal opens, focus moves into it; when it closes, focus returns to the trigger. The thing every JS dev forgets.

ARIA · where it helps, where it harms

The first rule of ARIA is: don't use ARIA if a native element works. Bad ARIA is worse than no ARIA — it overrides native behaviour with broken substitutes.

Wrong

<div role="button" tabindex="0" onclick> — reinventing a button. Doesn't handle Enter/Space. Doesn't work in form submission. Pure tax.

Right

<button>. Built-in keyboard behaviour, form participation, focus handling. Free.

Where ARIA is essential: dynamic content (live regions for status updates), complex widgets that don't exist in HTML (tab panels, comboboxes, trees), and labelling visual-only context (icon-only buttons need aria-label).

Tooling

  • axe-core — automated rule engine. Catches roughly 30–40% of issues. Run in CI; never trust as the only check.
  • Lighthouse accessibility — quick spot check. Same engine as axe under the hood.
  • Screen reader testing — NVDA (Windows, free), VoiceOver (macOS, built in). 30 minutes per major flow catches things axe can't see.
  • Keyboard-only testing — unplug your mouse for a day. The cheapest, most effective audit available.
  • User testing with assistive tech users — the only thing that catches real-world failure. Budget for it; don't pretend it's optional.
Accessibility is a competence, not a checklist. Tools catch the obvious; the rest is taught, reviewed, and shipped by people who care. If no one on the team is responsible for it, no one will be doing it.

External Integrations 101

Most production outages don't originate in your code; they originate in someone else's, propagated through an integration that wasn't hardened against their failure. Common Pitfall

Storing OAuth2 access tokens in localStorage. E · Integrations · Auth · Payments · Webhooks

Every integration is a contract with a system you don't control. The discipline is to assume it will fail, fail slowly, change its schema without telling you, throttle you unexpectedly, and occasionally just stop existing. Engineering for that posture is what separates integrations that survive launch from those that take you down with them.

Server-side vs client-side · the first decision

Where the call originates dictates every downstream concern: which credentials are exposed, what CORS rules apply, who pays for retries, and where the failure shows up to the user.

Server-side · default

Your backend calls the third-party. Credentials stay on the server. You control retries, timeouts, caching, rate limits. The user-visible API is your own.

Use when the call needs a secret (API key, OAuth client secret), or when you need to enrich/transform the response, or when the third-party can't be CORS-trusted by browsers.

Client-side · with care

The browser or mobile app calls the third-party directly. Faster (no extra hop), simpler (no backend code), but everything is public — every key in DevTools, every request loggable by any browser extension.

Use only when the API key is public by design (Google Maps with referer restriction), the data is non-sensitive, and the third-party explicitly supports browser CORS.

The common mistake: calling a "client-safe" API from the client without verifying the platform's restriction model. A Google Maps API key is public-safe only because Google enforces HTTP referer restrictions on it; the same approach with a Stripe secret key is a six-figure breach.

API gateway as integration mediator

Already covered as Layer 10 of the Deployment 101 architecture. In an integration context, the gateway plays a second role: it's the single point where outbound integrations cross your perimeter. Centralising this matters more than centralising inbound.

  • One outbound proxy — third-party calls go through one waypoint, not scattered across every service. You get audit logs, rate-limit budgets, and a single place to swap a vendor.
  • Egress allow-list — production services can only call domains on the allow-list. Compromised dependency tries to exfiltrate? Blocked at the gateway.
  • Secrets injection — services don't hold third-party keys; the gateway adds them on the way out. Rotate keys in one place.
  • Per-vendor circuit breakers — when Stripe degrades, isolate the blast radius to checkout. The rest of the system keeps running.

Authentication options · the menu

Every API on the planet uses one of these. Recognise the pattern; pick the right one; understand why the others are wrong for the case.

API key
The simplest

One static string per integration. Sent in a header (X-API-Key) or query param. Server-side only for any sensitive API. No expiry without manual rotation.

Basic Auth
Username + password, base64

Authorization: Basic base64(user:pass). HTTPS-only — base64 is encoding, not encryption. Common in legacy enterprise integrations and webhook receivers. Still in use; still mostly fine if scoped narrowly.

HMAC signed
Request signing

Client signs the request body and timestamp with a shared secret; server verifies. Used by AWS SigV4, Stripe webhook signatures, WhatsApp webhooks. Resistant to replay (timestamp window) and tampering (signature).

OAuth 2.0
Token-based, delegated

User grants permission; you receive an access token (short-lived) and refresh token (long-lived). Four common flows; each fits a different use case (below).

JWT
Self-contained tokens

JSON payload + signature in one string. Server validates without a DB lookup. Used as OAuth access tokens, internal service-to-service auth, single sign-on. Bearer JWTs are bearer tokens — leak = full access until expiry.

mTLS
Certificate-based

Both sides prove identity with X.509 certs. No tokens to leak; rotation handled by the cert lifecycle. Standard for high-value B2B integrations (payment networks, banking, healthcare).

OAuth 2.0 · the four flows that matter

  • Authorization Code + PKCE · the default for any app where a user logs in. User redirected to provider, returns with a one-time code, server (or PKCE-verifying client) exchanges code for tokens. PKCE replaces the old "client secret" requirement for public clients (mobile, SPA).
  • Client Credentials · service-to-service, no user. Two services authenticate themselves with a client_id + client_secret and get a token. Use for backend integrations where there's no human in the loop.
  • Device Code · for input-constrained devices (smart TV, CLI tool). Shows a code; user logs in on their phone; device polls until granted. The flow GitHub uses for gh auth login.
  • Implicit · deprecated. The old SPA flow that returned tokens directly in the URL fragment. Use Authorization Code + PKCE instead. If a vendor docs page still recommends Implicit, push back.

JWT · what's actually in the token

Three base64-encoded parts joined by dots: header.payload.signature. The payload is JSON claims. Standard claims to know:

a JWT payload, decoded
{
  "iss": "https://auth.example.com",   // issuer
  "sub": "user_abc123",                 // subject (user/service ID)
  "aud": "api.example.com",              // audience (who should accept it)
  "exp": 1764680400,                       // expiry (unix ts) — REQUIRED
  "iat": 1764676800,                       // issued at
  "scope": "read:orders write:orders",    // granted permissions
  "roles": ["customer"]
}
  • Always verify exp, iss, aud — the three claims that turn a JWT from "decoded JSON" into "authenticated token"
  • Algorithm — RS256 (asymmetric, signing key separate from verifying key) for anything cross-service. HS256 (shared secret) only when both sides are inside your trust boundary.
  • The alg: none attack — old libraries accepted JWTs with "alg": "none" as unsigned. Modern libraries reject it; verify yours does.
  • Refresh tokens are not JWTs — they should be opaque, server-side-revocable, and rotated on use. JWTs as refresh tokens = no revocation = leaked tokens valid for the full lifetime.

AD & enterprise SSO integrations

"AD integration" usually means one of three things depending on era and counterparty: classic on-prem Active Directory, modern Entra ID (formerly Azure AD), or a federation protocol (SAML, OIDC). The architect's first question on any AD ask: which one do they actually mean?

LDAP / AD bind
Legacy direct

Your app authenticates a user by binding to AD with their credentials over LDAP. Direct, simple, increasingly rare. Common in internal enterprise apps; almost extinct in customer-facing builds.

SAML 2.0
Enterprise SSO

Identity provider (Entra ID, Okta, ADFS) returns an XML SAML assertion via browser POST. Heavy, verbose, but every enterprise IdP speaks it. Pick this when the customer says "SSO" and is on a 2015-era stack.

OIDC
OAuth + identity

OpenID Connect layers identity on top of OAuth 2.0. The id_token JWT carries user identity claims. Modern default for any new build. All major IdPs (Entra, Okta, Auth0, Cognito, Keycloak) support it.

  • SCIM · System for Cross-domain Identity Management. The provisioning protocol — how the IdP creates, updates and deactivates users in your app automatically. Without SCIM, every leaver becomes a manual ticket. With SCIM, a deactivation in Entra propagates in minutes.
  • Just-in-time provisioning · the lightweight alternative — create the user on first SAML/OIDC login. Cheaper, but no automatic deactivation. Acceptable for low-stakes apps; not for regulated environments.
  • Group → role mapping · the IdP knows AD groups; your app knows roles. The mapping is the integration's actual policy surface. Document it explicitly per environment.
  • Multiple IdPs · enterprise customers want to bring their own. Plan for it from day one — an OIDC abstraction is much easier to add up front than to retrofit.

Worked example · Google Maps (client-side)

The canonical "safe to call from the browser" integration. Google enforces the security model on their side; you configure it correctly on yours.

  • Key restriction · in Google Cloud Console, restrict the key by HTTP referrer (your domains only) and by API (Maps JS, Places, Geocoding — only the ones you use). Unrestricted Maps keys are routinely scraped from public sites and used for free traffic until you get billed.
  • API choice · Maps JavaScript API for the visible map. Places API for autocomplete/search. Geocoding API for address-to-coordinates. Routes API for directions. Each is metered separately.
  • Cost discipline · cache geocoding results aggressively (addresses don't move). Debounce autocomplete (300ms typical). Set quotas in the console as guardrails — a misconfigured loop can bill thousands overnight.
  • Server-side alternative · for high-volume geocoding (batch imports, ETL), call the API server-side with a separate IP-restricted key. The client and server keys are different keys with different restrictions.

Worked example · WhatsApp Business API

Notification and conversational channels at scale. Meta's API is opinionated; the patterns are non-obvious if you've only worked with email or SMS.

  • Cloud API vs On-Premises · Cloud API is Meta-hosted (now the default). On-Premises is being deprecated. For new builds, Cloud API only.
  • Phone number setup · a dedicated phone number per WhatsApp Business Account. Verified through Meta Business Manager. The number can't be used elsewhere (regular WhatsApp, voice).
  • Outbound · templates only outside the 24h window · for the first message or messages sent >24h since the user last contacted you, you must use a pre-approved message template. Templates are reviewed by Meta and take 24–48h. Inside the 24h window, any message format.
  • Inbound · webhooks · Meta POSTs to your webhook endpoint for every message, status change, and event. Verify the X-Hub-Signature-256 header (HMAC-SHA256 over the body) on every request. Unsigned = forged.
  • Rate limits · tiered by account quality rating (Green / Yellow / Red). Too many user-initiated blocks → downgrade → throughput drops. Quality is a business metric, not just a technical one.
  • Idempotency · Meta retries webhooks if you don't 200 quickly. Dedupe by message ID server-side, or you'll process duplicates.

Payment gateways · the integration with the highest stakes

Payment integrations are different. Get any other API wrong and you write a fix; get this one wrong and you lose money or PCI scope. Two patterns dominate (with regional variants).

Stripe-style
PaymentIntent + SDK

Server creates a PaymentIntent, returns a client secret. Client SDK (Stripe.js / Elements) collects card details — they never touch your servers. Client confirms the intent; result delivered via webhook to server.

Razorpay-style (India)
Order create + checkout

Server creates an Order, returns the order ID. Client opens hosted Checkout (or SDK). On success, client returns a signed payload — server verifies the signature, then trusts the payment.

  • PCI scope minimisation · the goal is never to have card data touch your servers. Both patterns achieve this by collecting card details in a vendor-hosted iframe or SDK. If your code ever sees a PAN (card number), you're in PCI DSS Level 1/2 territory — a different operational reality.
  • Idempotency keys · always send an idempotency key on payment creates. Retries from network blips are common; without idempotency keys, you charge twice.
  • Webhook for truth, response for hint · the API response says "payment created"; the webhook says "payment captured." Always treat the webhook as authoritative. Provision orders on the webhook, not on the response.
  • Reconciliation · run a daily reconciliation job comparing your DB of payments to the gateway's reports. Catches: webhook drops, duplicate processing, refunds you didn't see. The cheapest insurance you have.
  • Refunds, partial captures, disputes · the state machine is bigger than "paid / not paid." Read the gateway's state diagram before designing the order model, not after.
  • Vendor choice · Stripe (global), Razorpay (India), Adyen (enterprise/global), PayU (LATAM, India), Worldline (EMEA enterprise). The integration shape is similar; the negotiating posture differs widely.

3PL · third-party logistics

For commerce builds, the shipping integration is usually a logistics aggregator that fronts dozens of carriers. The integration shape is consistent across vendors.

  • Rate shopping · POST a shipment manifest, receive rates from N carriers. Choose cheapest, fastest, or per-policy. Cache rates by route for short windows (rates change daily).
  • Label generation · POST a "buy this rate" call, receive a printable label (PDF or PNG) + tracking number. Async in some vendors; poll or webhook.
  • Tracking webhooks · the carrier sends status updates (picked up, in transit, out for delivery, delivered, returned). Normalise carrier-specific status codes to your own canonical set before storing.
  • Returns / RTO · in markets with high RTO rates (India: 25–35% on COD), the returns flow is as much code as the outbound flow. Plan for it from day one, not as a phase-2 feature.
  • Vendors · Shiprocket, Delhivery, EasyPost, Shippo, ShipStation. Aggregators trade convenience for margin — direct carrier integrations are cheaper at scale, expensive in dev time.

Webhooks · the inverse integration

Most integrations are you call them. Webhooks are they call you. The hardest integration to get right, because every assumption about your own infrastructure must hold under their retry behaviour.

The five rules of webhook receivers

  • Verify the signature on every request · HMAC-SHA256 over the raw body with a shared secret is the standard. Stripe, Meta, Shopify all use this. Never trust an unsigned webhook.
  • Respond fast — 200 within seconds, then process async · most providers time out at 5–10 seconds and retry. Queue the payload, return 200, process from the queue. Synchronous business logic in the handler is how you cause webhook storms.
  • Idempotency by event ID · providers retry. You'll receive the same event 2–N times. Dedupe by the provider's event ID — store seen IDs for a week.
  • No ordering guarantee · "order shipped" can arrive before "order paid." The handler must be commutative or have explicit ordering logic via timestamps/versioning.
  • Dead-letter queue · if processing fails after retries, the event goes to a DLQ for human review. Without one, failed events disappear silently.

When you send webhooks

If your system sends webhooks to other systems (you're the platform), the same rules apply in reverse:

  • Sign every payload · publish the signing key per consumer.
  • Retry with exponential backoff · 1, 2, 4, 8, 16, 32 minutes is a common schedule. Stop after 24 hours of failures and notify the consumer.
  • Include event ID and timestamp · the consumer needs them to dedupe and order.
  • Provide a replay endpoint · "give me all events between T1 and T2" so consumers can recover from extended outages.

Failure modes · making the perimeter hold

Every integration will fail. The question is whether it takes your system down with it.

Timeout
Bounded waiting

Every outbound call has an aggressive timeout — typically 2–5s for synchronous user flows, 30s for batch. The default in most HTTP libraries is infinity. Fix this on day one.

Retry + backoff
The right kind of stubborn

Exponential backoff with jitter. Cap at 3–5 retries for sync; longer for async. Only retry idempotent operations. Retrying a payment create without an idempotency key = double charge.

Circuit breaker
Stop hammering

After N consecutive failures, open the circuit — fail fast for the next M seconds. Periodically test with a single request (half-open). Standard pattern; libraries exist (Resilience4j, Polly).

Bulkhead
Contain the damage

Separate connection pools per integration. Stripe slow doesn't starve threads needed for WhatsApp. The cost of integrations sharing a pool is one outage taking down everything.

Contract testing & vendor SLAs

  • Contract tests · tools like Pact let you record the contract between your code and the vendor's API. When the vendor changes the response shape (and they will), your tests fail before production does.
  • Sandbox parity · vendor sandbox/test environments are not production. Bugs in the sandbox are not always bugs in production and vice versa. Plan a smoke test in production behind a kill-switch.
  • Vendor SLAs · read them. Most are 99.5% or 99.9%, which is 3.65h or 43m of permitted downtime per month. Your own SLO must accept that this can compound.
  • Multi-vendor for critical paths · for payments especially, having a secondary gateway you can fail over to is what turns a vendor outage from a P1 into a P3. Costs more in integration; saves you in incidents.
An integration isn't done when it works. It's done when it fails gracefully — when the vendor goes down at 3am on a Saturday and the rest of your system keeps running. That posture is engineered, not assumed.

Common Pitfall

Using git merge instead of git rebase for updating feature branches in a trunk-based workflow. Merge commits create a non-linear history. git bisect becomes unreliable; git blame becomes misleading; the merge commit hides the actual change behind a "Merge branch X into main" entry. Rebase to a linear history; squash to a logical commit.

R · Git · PRs · Branching · Promotion · Versioning

Release management starts in Git. Before there's a deploy pipeline, a rollback, or a hypercare plan, there's a branching strategy and a PR discipline. Get those right and most of release management gets easy; get them wrong and no amount of CI/CD tooling will save you.

Branching strategy · pick one, stick to it

There are essentially three patterns the industry uses. The right one depends on team size, deploy cadence, and how regulated the environment is.

Trunk-based
One long-lived branch

Everyone commits to main, behind feature flags for incomplete work. Short-lived branches (hours, not weeks) for individual PRs. Fits: high-velocity teams, mature CI, feature-flag infra. Default for SaaS.

GitHub Flow
Main + feature branches

main is always deployable. Branch off main for each feature, PR, merge back. No long-lived release branches. Fits: most agency work, mid-sized teams, weekly-to-monthly releases.

GitFlow
Multiple long-lived branches

main, develop, release/*, feature/*, hotfix/*. Complex but explicit. Fits: regulated environments, infrequent releases, multiple parallel versions in production. Wrong for most agency work — it's heavy ceremony for problems most teams don't have.

How to decide: deploy daily or more → trunk-based. Deploy weekly or biweekly → GitHub Flow. Deploy quarterly with audit gates → GitFlow. If you can't answer the deploy-cadence question, that's the first conversation to have.

Branch names & commit hygiene

  • Branch naming — type/ticket-id-short-description. Examples: feat/PROJ-1234-add-search, fix/PROJ-5678-cart-total, chore/upgrade-react-19. Predictable, sortable, traceable.
  • Conventional Commits — feat:, fix:, chore:, docs:, refactor:, test:, perf:, build:. Powers automated changelog and version bumping. Worth the small discipline.
  • Atomic commits — one logical change per commit. A revertable unit. Don't bundle "refactor + new feature + lint fix" into one commit; future-you will hate present-you when something goes wrong.
  • Commit body for non-trivial changes — why, not what. The diff shows what; the body shows the reasoning.

Pull requests · the unit of integration

A PR is the moment your code becomes everyone's code. The mechanics determine whether reviews are useful or theatre.

  • Size · under 400 lines changed is the empirical sweet spot. Above that, review quality collapses; reviewers skim instead of think. Big features → multiple small PRs behind a feature flag.
  • Draft early — open a draft PR as soon as the shape is there. Get directional feedback before investing in polish.
  • PR description as documentation — what changed, why, how to test, what was deliberately not done. The first commit message for the squash-merge.
  • Self-review before requesting review — open your own PR diff. You'll catch half the issues yourself.
  • One approval + green CI — the standard gate. Two approvals for sensitive areas (auth, payments, data migrations).
  • Review SLA — agree on it. "PRs reviewed within 1 business day" or the team gets stuck. Aging PRs are tech debt accumulating.

Branch protection rules

Configuration on the platform (GitHub, GitLab, Bitbucket) that enforces the policy the team agreed to.

  • Require PR before merge — no direct pushes to main. Ever.
  • Require status checks — CI green, security scans clean, coverage above floor.
  • Require approvals — at least one, typically. CODEOWNERS for sensitive paths automatically requests the right reviewers.
  • Dismiss stale reviews — when new commits land after approval, reset the approval. Avoids silent slipping of unrelated changes past review.
  • No force-push to main — ever, for any reason. History is the audit trail.
  • Signed commits — required in regulated environments. GPG or platform signing.
  • Linear history — squash-merge or rebase-merge only. Merge commits make git bisect harder than it needs to be.

Promotion · how a change moves from feature branch to production

Promotion is what turns a Git event into a customer-visible release. The pattern depends on the branching strategy.

Trunk-based promotion · every merge is a release candidate

Three principles regardless of strategy:

  • Build once, promote many — the same artefact moves through environments. Don't rebuild per environment; configuration differs, code does not.
  • Promotion gates are explicit — automated tests at the gate to stage; manual approval at the gate to prod (unless you're confident enough in automation to skip it).
  • Rollback is a first-class operation — deploying the previous artefact must be a button press, not a hotfix branch. If rolling back is harder than rolling forward, the system isn't actually safe.

Deploy patterns at production

Blue-green
Two production environments, one live

Deploy to the dark stack; smoke test; flip the router. Rollback = flip back. Doubles infra cost; halves rollback time.

Canary
Roll out to a small slice first

1% → 10% → 50% → 100%, with metrics watched at each step. Catches issues before full exposure. Requires good observability.

Feature flag
Code deployed, feature off

Decouple deploy from release. Flip the flag on for 1% of users; measure; expand. The pattern that makes trunk-based feasible.

Merge vs rebase · the argument resolved

Rebase your own branch

While the PR is open and only you've touched it. Keeps history linear, removes interleaved noise. The right move before you ask for review.

Merge to integrate

Squash-merge (collapse to one commit) or merge-commit (preserve the branch history) when bringing the PR into main. Don't rebase a shared branch — anyone else who pulled it gets a corrupted history.

Squash vs merge-commit at PR time: squash gives one tidy commit per PR (clean main history); merge-commit preserves intra-PR commits (richer history, more noise). Most teams pick squash and treat the PR description as the canonical message.

Versioning · what version are we on?

Two conventions matter. Pick the right one for the artefact.

SemVer · MAJOR.MINOR.PATCH

Libraries, APIs, SDKs. MAJOR breaks compatibility. MINOR adds capability. PATCH fixes bugs. Consumers can pin and predict.

CalVer · YYYY.MM.PATCH

Applications, SaaS, browser releases. The version tells you when it shipped. Useful when the question "is this from before or after X?" matters more than "is this compatible?"

  • Release tags — every deploy gets an immutable Git tag (v1.4.2 or 2026.03.05). Tag is the audit anchor.
  • Deployment tags — separate from release tags. "v1.4.2 → prod 2026-03-05T14:30" is a deployment event, not a code event.
  • Automated bumping — Conventional Commits + semantic-release (or equivalent) reads the commit history and bumps the version automatically. Removes the human "what version is this?" conversation.
  • Changelog generated from commits. Belongs in the release artefact, not in someone's email.

Hypercare & rollback windows

Release isn't done when the deploy completes. It's done after hypercare expires.

  • Hypercare window — defined per release. Typically 24–72 hours of heightened monitoring after a non-trivial change.
  • Named on-call — for the hypercare window, a named engineer is on the hook. Not "the team."
  • Rollback decision authority — agreed in advance. Who can push the button without a meeting? When is a meeting required?
  • Post-release verification — automated checks beyond "site is up." Business KPIs (conversions, signups) should be the strongest signal that the release is good.

4 · The ADR format

An Architecture Decision Record captures one significant decision in four mandatory fields. The status field is the decision's lifecycle. An ADR is never deleted — when a decision is reversed, the old ADR is marked Superseded and a new ADR records the reversal and its rationale.

Field
Content
Context
The forces at play. What constraint, requirement, or risk made this decision necessary. Written so a reader with no prior context understands why the problem exists.
Decision
The single chosen option, stated without hedging. Alternatives considered may be listed, but the chosen option is unambiguous. This is the sentence the PR reviewer checks implementation against.
Consequences
Trade-offs accepted. What becomes easier, what becomes harder, what new constraints are introduced. Negative consequences are mandatory — an ADR with no stated costs was not thought through.
Status
Proposed → decision drafted, under review  ·  Accepted → implementing PRs may proceed  ·  Superseded → replaced by ADR-NNN, reason stated
Common Pitfall

Writing ADRs after the decision has been implemented. An ADR written in retrospect cannot influence the decision — it is archaeology, not governance. The ADR must be written before the implementing PR is merged. The PR links to the ADR; the reviewer checks that the implementation matches the decision.

Before / After
Before

Architect communicates design decisions verbally in stand-up. Not recorded. A new engineer joins in week 6 and re-litigates a decision made in week 1. Two sprint-planning sessions consumed by a debate that was already settled.

After

ADR log in repo. New engineer reads it during onboarding. Decision is closed, not re-opened. Context survives team rotation.

1 · Pipeline stage anatomy

A Cloud Manager full-stack pipeline runs nine stages in a fixed sequence. Failure at any stage stops the run — downstream stages never execute.

flowchart TD
  A([Start]) --> B[Build]
  B --> C[Unit Test]
  C --> D[Code Quality Gate]
  D -->|fail| A
  D -->|pass| E[Deploy Dev]
  E --> F[UI Test]
  F --> G[Perf Test]
  G --> H[Deploy Stage]
  H --> I{Approval Gate}
  I -->|approved| J[Deploy Prod]
  I -->|rejected| A
  • Build — Maven build of the full AEM project. Produces the combined dispatcher and AEM content package artifacts. Failure here is a compilation or packaging error.
  • Unit Test — JUnit tests run against the compiled code, not against a running AEM instance. Fast. No AEM required. Every business-logic class should have coverage here.
  • Code Quality Gate — SonarQube scan of the compiled code. Evaluates blocker/critical rule violations, code coverage floor, and security hotspots. This is the gate that kills the most pipelines. It cannot be overridden by a manager — only by fixing the code or updating the quality profile with documented justification.
  • Deploy Dev — the approved artifact is deployed to the Dev AEM environment. OSGi bundles activate, Sling mappings resolve, dispatcher rules propagate. Manual state on Dev is wiped.
  • UI Test — Selenium or Cypress tests run against the live Dev environment. Tests must be committed in ui.tests/. An empty test suite passes; no suite at all triggers a warning.
  • Perf Test — synthetic load test against Dev. Adobe measures page-load response times and error rates. Configurable thresholds; default is ≤3s p95 response time, ≤2% error rate.
  • Deploy Stage — same artifact deployed to Stage. Stage must be a production-representative environment — same dispatcher config, same CDN headers, same replication topology.
  • Approval Gate — a named human must click Approve in the CM UI. This is where DEPT® recommends a mandatory sign-off from the client lead, not just the tech lead.
  • Deploy Prod — artifact promotes to production. Adobe's blue-green switch happens here (see §4).

2 · Quality gate definitions

The CM code quality gate runs three independent checks. All three must pass for the gate to clear.

  • SonarQube blocker / critical rules — zero blockers permitted. Critical violations are rated; the default quality profile blocks on any critical security rule and on critical reliability rules (null-pointer paths, unclosed resources). DEPT® recommendation: run the same SonarQube profile in the local IDE on every PR — do not learn about blockers for the first time in a CM run.
  • Code coverage floor — new code must meet a minimum line-coverage percentage. Adobe's default is 50% for new lines. Projects should set this to ≥70% in the custom quality profile; the cost of enforcing it is far lower than the cost of a regression in untested code on a live site.
  • Performance score threshold — the perf test stage enforces a maximum error rate (default 2%) and a p95 response time ceiling (default 3 000 ms). Both are configurable per pipeline in the CM UI. Set them to the values your SLA actually demands — not Adobe's defaults.
Why This Matters

CM pipeline constraints are architecture inputs. A monorepo where multiple teams share one CM pipeline creates deployment coupling — each team's release cadence is gated by every other team's quality gate. Separate pipelines per team, or a monorepo split, must be decided in the architecture phase.

  • Deployable by any team member with CM Developer role — no Java expertise required.

4 · Canary and blue-green mechanics in AEMaaCS

AEMaaCS uses a managed blue-green deployment for the Author and Publish tiers. Architects do not configure this — Adobe operates it — but the behaviour has direct implications for deployment strategy.

  • Two parallel environments — Adobe maintains a "blue" and a "green" version of each tier. The CM deploy step activates the new version on the inactive slot, then flips the traffic switch.
  • Zero-downtime deploy — during the switch, in-flight requests complete on the old slot. New requests hit the new slot. The window is seconds, not minutes.
  • OSGi bundle activation matters — if a bundle fails to activate on the new slot, the switch does not occur. The pipeline reports a deployment failure, and the old slot remains live. This is the most common cause of "deploy completed but site looks wrong."
  • Canary is not yet available as a self-service feature in AEMaaCS — traffic splitting to a percentage of users requires Fastly or Akamai rules at the CDN layer, outside CM. Architect this explicitly if percentage rollout is a requirement.
  • Content compatibility window — blue-green means the new code and the old code will briefly serve the same content repository. Any migration that breaks backward compatibility (removing a JCR property the old code reads) must be executed in two pipeline runs: first remove the old code's dependency, then remove the property.

5 · Pipeline failure triage guide

A CM pipeline failure produces output in three different places. Looking at the wrong one wastes 30 minutes.

  • Build failure — open the CM build log directly in the pipeline run detail page. It is a Maven log. Search for [ERROR]. The first occurrence is the root cause; everything after is cascade. Common causes: missing dependency in pom.xml, compilation error in recently merged branch, packaging rule violation in the FileVault filter.
  • Unit test failure — the CM UI surfaces a JUnit XML report. Read the test class name and the assertion message — not the stack trace first. If the assertion is unclear, open the test source. Do not re-run the pipeline to see if it passes; a flaky unit test is a bug.
  • Code quality gate failure — navigate to SonarQube directly (the CM UI links to the project). Filter by "New Code" and sort by severity. Blockers first. Read the rule explanation — SonarQube's rule pages are detailed. If a rule fires incorrectly on a known pattern, suppress it with @SuppressWarnings("squid:SXXXX") and a comment explaining why, not silently.
  • Deploy failure — the error is in the AEM error log, not the CM log. SSH access is not available in AEMaaCS; use the AEM Developer Console (available at /system/console on Author) or the Cloud Manager Logs Download to pull the error.log from the failing tier. Search for BundleException or ERROR with the bundle symbolic name.
  • UI test failure — the CM UI attaches screenshots and a video recording of the Selenium run. Watch the video before reading the stack trace; the visual failure is usually self-explanatory. Common cause: selector changed because a component template was updated without updating the test.
  • Perf test failure — the CM perf report shows per-URL response times. Identify the slowest URL, then check the AEM request log for that URL. Common causes: uncached dispatcher rule, query without an Oak index, synchronous external API call in the render path.

6 · Security scanning

CM integrates two security scanning mechanisms. Both run automatically; neither requires separate configuration.

  • SAST via SonarQube — the code quality gate includes SonarQube's security ruleset. It identifies injection risks, insecure cryptography, hardcoded credentials, and OWASP Top 10 patterns in Java source. Security hotspots require human review — CM does not block on hotspots by default, but DEPT® sets the quality profile to block on unreviewed hotspots rated Critical or Higher.
  • Dependency CVE scan via CM Security Reports — Cloud Manager generates a content package security report on every full-stack pipeline run. It lists OSGi bundles with known CVEs in their embedded dependencies. The report is available under the pipeline execution detail. Unresolved Critical CVEs must be tracked in the project's risk register and resolved within the SLA agreed with the client's security team — typically 30 days for Critical, 90 days for High.
  • What CM does not cover — runtime application security (WAF, DDoS, bot management) sits at the CDN layer, outside CM. Infrastructure penetration testing is a separate engagement. Do not conflate CM security reports with a full security audit.

7 · Rollback mechanics

AEMaaCS rollback is re-promotion of the last successful pipeline execution — not a code revert, not a package restore.

  • Navigate to the pipeline in CM, select the last successful execution, and click Re-deploy. CM re-runs the deploy stage of that execution's artifact against the current production environment. Build, test, and quality stages do not re-run — the artifact was already validated.
  • Re-deploy takes the same time as a production deploy, typically 10–20 minutes for a full AEM tier swap.
  • Code revert in Git does not roll back production — it creates a new commit that will be deployed on the next pipeline run. Do not confuse a Git revert with a rollback.
  • Content is not rolled back. If the deployment included a content migration (JCR node changes, Oak index rebuild), those changes remain. Architect rollback-safe migrations: additive first, destructive only after the new code is confirmed stable.
  • The CM re-deploy button is only available if a successful execution exists for that pipeline. If the first production deploy of a project fails, there is no previous execution to re-promote — fix-forward is the only option.
Common Pitfall

Treating CM-Dev as a persistent development environment. CM-Dev is reset on every pipeline run — any manually applied OSGi configuration, any manually modified dispatcher rule, any manually uploaded package is gone on the next deploy. All configuration must be in code, committed, and deployed through the pipeline.

1 · Data layer to Analytics variable mapping — the Engineering/Analytics contract

The data layer is the single authoritative input to Adobe Analytics. Every eVar, prop, and event ultimately traces back to a value that either existed in the data layer or was derived from it inside Launch. This means the data layer is not an analytics artefact — it is a product engineering contract that the analytics team depends on absolutely.

Define the contract as a typed schema, version it alongside the codebase, and treat breaking changes to the data layer the same as breaking changes to an API. The mapping from data layer keys to AA variables (eVarN, propN, eventN) belongs in a Solution Design Reference (SDR) — a spreadsheet or Confluence table that is the living source of truth for every implementation decision.

The SDR must contain: the data layer key, the AA variable it maps to, the allocation model, the expiration, the scope (hit / visit / visitor), the business owner, and the date last modified. Without this document, every analyst who joins the team re-audits the implementation from scratch.

Enforce the contract at the Launch rule level — data elements read from window.digitalData or window.dataLayer exclusively. No Launch rule should scrape the DOM or read arbitrary JavaScript globals. DOM scraping breaks silently on every front-end refactor; a stable data layer API does not.

2 · Report suite strategy — global suite vs virtual report suites vs multi-suite tagging

Why This Matters

Report suite strategy is an architecture decision. Switching from multi-suite to a global suite requires re-implementing all tagging, rebuilding all dashboards, and retraining all analysts. There is no incremental migration path — it is a full reimplementation. Get it right in discovery; the cost of getting it wrong is measured in months, not sprints.

Three patterns exist. Choose one per engagement based on business structure, compliance requirements, and analyst team topology.

Global suite + virtual report suites (VRS). A single report suite receives all hits. VRS filters segment the data for brand teams, regions, or product lines. This is the correct default for a single brand operating across regions or a multi-brand organisation that needs cross-brand attribution. Analyst permissions are controlled at the VRS level. Processing rules and VISTA rules are written once. The tradeoff: a single configuration error affects all brands simultaneously.

Multi-suite tagging. Each hit fires to two or more report suites — typically a global rollup suite and a brand-specific suite. Useful when brand analytics teams need their own eVar/prop namespace and cannot share one with other brands. The cost: every processing rule must be configured independently in each suite; configuration drift is inevitable without tight governance. Use only when brand teams have hard autonomy requirements.

Standalone brand suites (no global). Each brand owns an independent report suite with no shared tagging. Cross-brand analysis is impossible in AA — it requires CJA or a data warehouse. Appropriate for holding companies with fully independent brands. Do not use when the business asks cross-brand questions even occasionally.

3 · Processing rules governance — ordering, version control, dev suite testing

Processing rules transform, copy, and set variable values at collection time — before data is written to the report suite. They execute sequentially, top to bottom, and each rule operates on the state left by all rules above it. This sequential dependency is the primary source of silent data quality failures in AA implementations.

The governance model DEPT® applies: processing rules are owned by the Analytics Architect, not the Analytics team at large. Changes require a pull request against the rule export JSON (see Agency Tip below). Rules are numbered with a three-digit prefix in their names — 010_set_pagename, 020_copy_product_sku — so the intended order is self-documenting in the admin UI. No rule is deployed to production without first being tested in a dedicated development report suite using Charles Proxy or Requestly to redirect image requests.

Common Pitfall

Processing rules that reference variables set by other processing rules without accounting for execution order. A rule at position 3 reads a variable set by a rule at position 7. The result is always empty — a silent data quality failure that shows up only in specific traffic segments and takes hours to diagnose without version-controlled rule exports.

Agency Tip

DEPT® field pattern — export all processing rules as JSON at the end of each sprint and commit to the project repository. Processing rules are configuration-as-code; without version control, rollback requires manual recreation from screenshots. With version control, rollback is a copy-paste into the AA admin UI.

4 · VISTA rules — when to use, when not to

VISTA (Visitor Identification, Segmentation, and Transformation Architecture) rules run server-side at Adobe's data collection layer, before processing rules. They are permanent — a VISTA rule that modifies a hit rewrites the stored record. There is no undo. They are also billed separately from the base AA contract.

Use VISTA when: you need to route hits to different report suites based on hit content (suite routing); you need to manipulate data that is not accessible via processing rules (e.g., the raw query string before URL decoding); or you need a transformation that must be irreversible for compliance reasons (e.g., stripping PII from the stored record before it reaches any report suite).

Do not use VISTA when: the transformation is something processing rules can accomplish — processing rules are free, configurable by the client, and reversible. Do not use VISTA for variable copying, string concatenation, or conditional logic that belongs in Launch or processing rules. Every VISTA rule is an Adobe Professional Services engagement; account for lead time and cost in project planning.

The test for VISTA: ask "if we need to undo this in six months, what happens?" If the answer is "we can't — the raw data is gone," then VISTA is appropriate and the decision must be documented in the SDR with business owner sign-off. If the answer is "we just change a processing rule," then VISTA is the wrong tool.

5 · eVar and prop allocation strategy — attribution model as a business question

Every eVar has an allocation model (Last Touch, First Touch, Linear, Participation) and an expiration (hit, visit, visitor, custom date, or never). These are not technical defaults — they are answers to a business question: when a conversion happens, which touchpoint gets credit? The wrong allocation model produces directionally incorrect reports, not just inaccurate ones.

Last Touch gives 100% credit to the most recent value before conversion. Use for campaign tracking where the final click drives action — internal search term that preceded purchase, last product viewed. Do not use for content or channel variables where the full journey matters.

Linear distributes credit equally across all values in the conversion window. Use when no single touchpoint is more important than others — multi-step content sequences, onboarding flows, drip campaign attribution where you want to see the full sequence rather than only the last step.

Participation gives 100% credit to every value that participated in the conversion path. Use for binary questions: "did this content appear in any successful conversion path?" Participation inflates totals (they will not sum to conversion count) but answers the right question for content contribution analysis.

Props (traffic variables) have no persistence and no allocation — they capture the value at the moment of the hit only. Use props for pathing (Next Page Flow, Previous Page Flow) and for high-cardinality dimensions where visitor-level persistence is not needed. Props do not convert; if you need a value to participate in conversion reports, use an eVar.

Naming convention is enforcement. Every eVar and prop in the SDR must carry a human-readable name in the AA admin UI — eVar15 = Internal Search Term (Last Touch, Visit). Anonymous variable names (eVar15 with no label) mean the next analyst cannot audit the implementation without interviewing the original team. Lock down variable naming changes behind the same governance process as rule changes.

6 · Classification sets — migrating from SAINT, governance model

Classifications add lookup dimensions to existing variables — a SKU eVar gets Product Name, Category, and Margin Tier without those values ever being sent in the beacon. Adobe has deprecated the SAINT (SiteCatalyst Attribute Importing and Naming Tool) FTP import workflow and replaced it with Classification Sets, the current architecture.

Classification Sets are managed in the AA Admin UI under Data Management. Each set defines the source variable, the lookup keys, and the classification columns. Import is via file upload (tab-delimited) or the Classifications API. The critical governance requirement: classification data is owned by a business system (ERP, PIM, CRM) — not by the analytics team. Establish an automated feed from the source system rather than manual uploads. Manual uploads create stale classification data the moment the source system changes.

Migration from SAINT: export all existing SAINT files, validate key coverage against current traffic (keys not in the SAINT file produce unclassified rows — find and backfill them before migrating), create the equivalent Classification Sets, and establish the automated import pipeline before decommissioning the SAINT process. Do not run both in parallel longer than one sprint — divergence between SAINT and Classification Sets in the same report suite produces inconsistent dimension values that are invisible to analysts without a schema audit.

7 · Data feeds and data warehouse — when Analytics data needs to leave Adobe's ecosystem

Analysis Workspace and the Reporting API answer questions within Adobe's UI. When the business needs to join AA data with non-Adobe data, build custom models, or retain raw hit-level data beyond Adobe's retention window, the data must leave the ecosystem.

Data Feeds deliver raw, hit-level data as flat files (tab-delimited, gzip) to an S3 bucket or SFTP endpoint on a defined schedule (hourly or daily). Each file contains one row per hit with every column from the report suite schema — including post-processed values after processing rules and VISTA. Data Feeds are the right choice for teams that need to build a data warehouse or lake from AA hit data, run custom attribution models, or integrate with BI tools (Databricks, Snowflake, BigQuery) at the row level.

Data Warehouse runs SQL-like queries against the AA data store and delivers results as Excel or CSV files, usually to S3. It handles datasets larger than Analysis Workspace can render and supports custom calculated columns. Use Data Warehouse for large ad-hoc exports (full year of session-level data for a cohort), regulatory audit exports, or one-time migration snapshots. Do not use Data Warehouse for recurring operational reporting — that belongs in Analysis Workspace or the Reporting API.

Both products are governed by the AA data retention setting (default 25 months). If the business requires raw hit data beyond 25 months, Data Feeds must be running before the retention window closes. There is no backfill option — data not exported before the retention cutoff is gone permanently.

8 · AA-to-CJA migration path — what to keep, rebuild, and abandon

Customer Journey Analytics (CJA) is not an upgrade to Adobe Analytics — it is a different product with a different data model, built on Adobe Experience Platform datasets. The migration decision is architectural: it requires budget, a data engineering workstream, and analyst retraining. Most organisations run AA and CJA in parallel indefinitely rather than fully migrating.

What to keep in AA: real-time operational dashboards (CJA latency is minutes, not seconds); any workflow dependent on processing rules or VISTA that has not been replicated in AEP data prep; and any integration with AA-native products (Target via Analytics for Target, AAM segments via audience publishing) until those integrations are rebuilt on the AEP connector.

What to rebuild in CJA: cross-channel attribution (online + offline + CRM + call center in a single Analysis Workspace panel — this is CJA's primary value proposition and cannot be replicated in AA); long-window cohort analysis (AA workspace is limited in cohort depth; CJA operates on full historical datasets in AEP); and any report that requires stitching anonymous and authenticated sessions across devices.

What to abandon: prop-based pathing built for session-scoped traffic analysis — CJA's object-array data model makes traditional AA pathing reports obsolete; SAINT classification files (CJA uses AEP lookup datasets, which are schema-validated and version-controlled by default); and custom VISTA rules whose logic can be handled by AEP data prep transformations at ingestion time.

The migration prerequisite that teams consistently underestimate: the AEP Web SDK must be deployed and validated before CJA receives usable data. Teams that try to migrate CJA on top of an AppMeasurement implementation via the Analytics source connector will spend the first three months debugging schema mismatches rather than building analysis. Deploy the Web SDK first, validate data parity between AppMeasurement and Web SDK for a minimum of two billing periods, then light up the CJA connection.

2 · Data view governance

A CJA data view is the configuration layer between the raw datasets in a connection and the analyst in Analysis Workspace. It defines which XDM fields are exposed as dimensions or metrics, how those components are named, which attribution model applies to each metric, and what the default date range and session timeout are. Every data view decision is a governance decision — it determines what the analyst can see and what they cannot.

The four governance responsibilities that belong to the architect:

  • Field mapping — every exposed XDM field must have a human-readable name, a description, and a declared data type; unexposed fields must be explicitly excluded, not just ignored
  • Metric definition — each metric must declare the XDM event or field it counts, whether it is a count, sum, or distinct-count, and which attribution model applies (first touch, last touch, linear, participation, or custom)
  • Attribution model standardisation — the same business metric (e.g., "Revenue") must not appear in the data view twice with different attribution models under similar names; downstream analysts will use both and produce contradictory numbers
  • Component curation — a data view with every XDM field exposed is not a data view, it is a schema dump; curate to the components analysts actually use and document the rationale for every inclusion and exclusion
Why This Matters

Data view governance is an architect responsibility. A data view with 200 unmapped fields, duplicate metrics with different attribution models, and no component documentation is a liability. Analysts cannot trust data they cannot verify; an unverifiable metric drives wrong business decisions. The architect owns the data view schema. An analyst curator owns the component catalogue. Neither role is optional — without both, the data view degrades into the same unmaintainable state as the eVar spreadsheet it replaced.

3 · Cross-channel stitching

Stitching is CJA's mechanism for producing a person-based view of the customer journey across authenticated and unauthenticated sessions. Without stitching, CJA analyses events by device identifier — the same as Adobe Analytics. With stitching, it reprocesses historical anonymous events and assigns them to the authenticated identity once a login is observed, producing a continuous person record across channels.

Two stitching modes:

Field-based stitching (deterministic)

Uses a persistent authenticated ID (ECID, CRM ID, email hash) already present in every event. When the ID is absent — anonymous sessions — those events remain unstitched. Simple to configure; produces high-confidence joins; does not backfill anonymous pre-login behaviour.

Use when · authenticated rate is high (>50%) and the anonymous pre-login journey is not analytically significant.

Graph-based stitching (probabilistic)

Uses the AEP Identity Service identity graph to infer that anonymous device events belong to an authenticated person, based on device co-occurrence patterns and declared identity signals. Backfills anonymous history. More powerful; requires a healthy identity graph; introduces inference error rate that must be measured.

Use when · anonymous-to-authenticated journey analysis is required and the identity graph is well-populated.

Identity namespace configuration requirements:

  • Every dataset included in the CJA connection must declare which XDM field carries the person ID and which identity namespace it belongs to
  • The chosen namespace must be consistent across all datasets — mixing ECID-primary on web with CRM-ID-primary on CRM creates join failures that produce inflated person counts
  • Privacy implications are non-trivial: graph-based stitching infers linkages that users may not have explicitly consented to; the data view must be scoped to consented data only, and GDPR/CCPA right-to-erasure applies to the stitched graph, not just the source records
  • Stitching reprocesses data; a change to the stitching configuration invalidates historical stitched records and triggers a full reprocess — plan for 24–72 hour latency impact on backfill workloads

4 · Calculated metrics governance

CJA calculated metrics are analyst-authored formula metrics built on top of base metrics in the data view. They are powerful — any ratio, conditional count, or cross-dataset formula is expressible. They are also the primary vector through which data view trust degrades: without governance, a workspace accumulates dozens of calculated metrics with overlapping definitions, different attribution models, no owners, and no validation dates.

The DEPT® metric governance standard:

  • Naming convention — prefix identifies scope: [BU] for business-unit-specific, [GLOBAL] for cross-BU; suffix identifies attribution: _LC last-click, _LIN linear; example: [GLOBAL] Revenue_LC
  • Ownership — every metric has a declared owner (role, not person name); the owner is responsible for validation and deprecation
  • Change management — calculated metrics used in published dashboards are frozen; changes require a new metric with a versioned name, not an edit to the existing metric
  • Deprecation policy — metrics unused for 90 days, or with no declared owner, enter a 30-day deprecation notice period before deletion
Agency Tip

DEPT® field pattern — establish a metric registry in Confluence: each CJA calculated metric has an owner, a definition, an attribution model, a validation date, and a deprecation policy. Metrics without owners are sunset within 90 days. This prevents the calculated metric proliferation that makes data views unmaintainable after 12 months. The registry costs one sprint to establish and saves one re-implementation project every 18 months.

5 · Real-time personalisation loop

The full-loop personalisation architecture is the primary business justification for the Adobe Experience Cloud stack. 6 · CJA vs AA reporting parity checklist

CJA does not reproduce every Adobe Analytics report. The architectural differences — XDM schema versus eVar/prop, person-based versus hit-based, data view versus report suite — mean that some AA constructs have no direct CJA equivalent, and some CJA capabilities have no AA equivalent. Clients migrating from AA to CJA must audit their report inventory before decommissioning AA.

AA reports CJA cannot reproduce directly

  • Real-time reports (sub-1-minute) — CJA streaming has ~1-minute latency; AA real-time reports can approach 20-second latency with legacy hit processing
  • Classification rule builder — AA classifications must be replaced by lookup datasets in AEP joined at the CJA connection layer
CJA capabilities AA cannot match
  • Cross-channel person journey — joining web, app, call centre, and offline events on a single person record
  • SQL-accessible data — CJA connections query the AEP data lake; data scientists can access the same data via Query Service
  • Unlimited variables — no eVar or prop limits; every XDM field is a potential dimension
  • Flexible session definition — session timeout and context-aware session rules are configurable per data view, not fixed at the report suite level

7 · CJA data latency model

CJA latency is not a single number — it is the sum of latencies across four stages: SDK event firing, AEP ingestion, CJA connection sync, and Analysis Workspace query execution. Each stage is independently configurable and independently licenced. The overall latency an analyst experiences is the worst of the four.

Batch ingestion path

AEP ingestion latency: batch datasets (CRM, offline, call centre) land in the data lake on a configured schedule — typically hourly or daily. CJA connection sync: batch connections poll the data lake on a 60–90 minute cycle. Total observable latency: 2–3 hours for batch-sourced data. Suitable for daily and weekly trend reporting; not suitable for campaign-day dashboards or live ops monitoring.

Streaming ingestion path

AEP ingestion latency: Web SDK and Mobile SDK events land in AEP via streaming ingestion in under 60 seconds. CJA connection sync: streaming connections sync approximately every 1 minute (requires streaming ingestion licence add-on). Total observable latency: 1–3 minutes for web and mobile events. Adequate for near-real-time campaign monitoring; not equivalent to Adobe Analytics' 2–20 second latency.

Common Pitfall

Assuming CJA latency is equivalent to Adobe Analytics. CJA batch connections have up to 90-minute latency. Streaming connections reduce this to near-real-time but require additional AEP configuration and a streaming ingestion licence. A client dashboard built on CJA and presented as "real-time" to the business will produce escalations when the 90-minute lag is discovered during a campaign launch. Agree on the latency model in writing before the first executive dashboard is built.

Near-real-time implications for operations teams:

  • Campaign launch monitoring — streaming ingestion with a CJA streaming connection gives sufficient freshness for hourly campaign performance checks but not for minute-by-minute operations rooms; use AEP Query Service for sub-minute ad-hoc queries
  • Anomaly detection — CJA's built-in anomaly detection runs on the data view cadence; streaming data enables same-hour anomaly alerts; batch data produces next-day anomaly alerts
  • AA as a complementary real-time source — during the migration period, Adobe Analytics can serve as the real-time dashboard source while CJA handles cross-channel and person-based analysis; decommissioning AA before establishing streaming ingestion creates a monitoring gap

Journey types · event-triggered, segment-based, batch — with use-case mapping

AJO supports three fundamentally different journey entry mechanisms. Choosing the wrong type for the use case is the most common architectural error — and it is expensive to unwind after go-live.

Event-triggered
React to a moment

A specific XDM event — cart abandonment, order placed, support ticket opened — fires and the journey begins for that profile within seconds. Latency is bounded by AEP event ingestion (<1 min for streaming). Use for: transactional flows, real-time engagement, behavioural triggers. Entry is individual and timestamped. Re-entry rules govern whether the same profile can re-enter the journey.

Segment-based
React to a state

Profiles enter when they qualify for or exit a segment — "high-value customer," "lapsed user," "loyalty tier upgrade." Entry fires on segment membership change, not on a discrete event. Latency is bounded by segment evaluation cadence (streaming segments: near-real-time; batch segments: up to 24h). Use for: lifecycle programmes, tier changes, re-engagement. The segment definition is the entry contract — change it and the journey audience changes with it.

Batch (read audience)
Scheduled audience send

A snapshot of an AEP audience is loaded and every profile in it enters the journey at a scheduled time. No real-time trigger — this is the AJO equivalent of a campaign send. Use for: newsletters, scheduled promotions, regulatory notices, one-time communications to a defined audience. Do not use this where real-time responsiveness is required — a batch journey cannot react faster than its schedule.

  • Re-entry rules — for event-triggered journeys, define explicitly whether a profile can re-enter: never, after journey completes, or always. The default is restrictive; a purchase-confirmation journey that allows re-entry on every purchase is correct behaviour. A welcome journey that allows re-entry is a defect.
  • Audience size vs journey type — batch journeys can handle millions of profiles at schedule time. Event-triggered journeys scale to the event volume. Segment-based journeys depend on how many profiles change segment membership in a window. Size the Channel sandbox accordingly before launch.
  • Journey versioning — every change to a live journey produces a new version. Old profiles continue on their version; new entrants use the current version. This is not optional; it is AJO's safety model. It also means journey debt accumulates. Archive aggressively.

XDM event schema governance · the contract between Engineering and AJO

The XDM event schema is the interface between the data layer (owned by Engineering) and the journey layer (owned by Marketing/CX Operations). It is a contract. Both sides must agree to its terms before either side builds against it.

The governance model has four requirements:

  • Schema-first — the XDM schema is finalised and versioned in the AEP Schema Registry before any journey references it. Not "draft" — finalised. A draft schema is not a contract.
  • Field-level dependency tracking — every journey entry condition and personalisation field must be mapped to a specific XDM field path. This is the "journey dependency map" (see Agency Tip). Without it, a field rename in the data layer silently kills a journey.
  • Versioned schema changes — breaking schema changes (field removal, rename, type change) require a new schema version and a mandatory review of all journeys that reference the affected fields. Non-breaking additions (new optional fields) do not. Define the policy in writing before Sprint 1.
  • Schema change notification — Engineering must notify AJO/CX Operations before merging any schema change that affects a live journey. The lead time must accommodate the 24-hour journey republication evaluation window.

Why This Matters

AJO schema governance is an architect responsibility. A journey that references an XDM field that no longer exists — because the data layer was updated without versioning — silently fails. No error, no fallback, no alert. Users fall out of the journey without explanation and without the business knowing. The XDM contract is what prevents this class of incident.

Frequency capping architecture · suppression lists, fatigue rules, compliance

Frequency capping in AJO is not a marketing preference — it is compliance architecture. GDPR, CAN-SPAM, TCPA, and equivalent regulations impose legally binding constraints on outreach volume and opt-out handling. Design this before you build journeys, not after.

  • Global frequency rules — AJO supports business-unit-wide caps (e.g., max 3 emails per profile per week across all journeys). These are set at the sandbox level and apply across all channels unless channel-specific rules override. Confirm the cap values with legal — not with the email team.
  • Journey-level capping — individual journeys can declare their own frequency rules, which stack with global rules. The most restrictive rule wins. A profile at the global weekly cap will not receive a journey email even if the journey's own cap has not been reached.
  • Suppression lists — three types: global suppressions (hard bounces, unsubscribes — never contact again); regulatory suppressions (litigation hold, financial communication restrictions); campaign-level suppressions (exclude existing customers from acquisition journey). All three are maintained in AEP as datasets and referenced by AJO at journey-entry evaluation. They are not opt-in exclusions — they are mandatory pre-filters.
  • Fatigue rules (AI-powered) — AJO's AI send-time optimisation can suppress sends when the model predicts a profile has entered communication fatigue. This is additive to hard caps, not a replacement. Do not rely on AI fatigue detection as a substitute for explicit cap rules — it is probabilistic, not deterministic.
  • Opt-out handling — email and SMS opt-outs must be honoured within 10 business days (CAN-SPAM), with many jurisdictions requiring immediate effect. AJO's unsubscribe handling updates the AEP consent dataset; journeys read consent at entry and at send time. A profile can re-enter a journey after opting out if they re-opt-in — but the journey must re-check consent at every send node, not just at entry.
Common Pitfall

Building journeys before the XDM schema is finalised. Schema changes after journey deployment require journey republication — a 24-hour evaluation period before the fix is live in production. In that window, users either receive the wrong experience or fall out of the journey. Schema-first, journey-second is not a preference — it is a delivery constraint.

AJO + AEM integration pattern · content fragment publication to AJO offer catalogue

The canonical DEPT® integration pattern treats AEM as the system of record for all content — including the fragments that AJO delivers through journeys. AJO never authors content; it selects and dispatches content authored elsewhere.

  • Step 1 · Fragment authoring in AEM — content authors create Content Fragments using a model that maps to the AJO offer payload schema: headline (text), body (rich text), CTA label (text), CTA URL (text), image reference (asset path), offer metadata (tags, expiry, placement IDs). The model is agreed between Engineering and CX Operations before any fragments are created.
  • Step 2 · Publication pipeline — AEM's publish workflow includes a custom step (OSGi bundle or workflow launcher) that invokes the Adobe Offer Library API to create or update an offer in AJO when a fragment is published. Fragment unpublication triggers offer status change to "archived" — not deletion, to preserve reporting history.
  • Step 3 · Offer catalogue in AJO — offers are organised by placement (email banner, in-app modal, web hero, SMS body) and by collection (product line, campaign, seasonal). Collections feed the Decision Management ranking engine. Placements define the content shape the channel expects.
  • Step 4 · Journey content reference — AJO journey nodes reference a placement ID, not a specific offer. Decision Management selects the ranked offer at runtime based on profile eligibility, priority, and capping rules. This means the same journey node can serve different content to different profiles without journey modification.
  • Versioning consideration — when an AEM fragment is updated and republished, the corresponding offer in AJO is updated in place. If the update changes the offer payload schema (e.g., adds a new required field), any journey template that does not reference that field will silently omit it. Fragment schema changes require a journey template review — add this to the dependency map.

Decision Management (Offer Decisioning) · ranked offers integrating with journey branching

Decision Management is the AJO component that determines which offer a profile sees when a journey node requests content for a placement. It is a ranked eligibility engine, not a random selector. Getting the ranking model right determines the commercial outcome of every personalised touchpoint.

Eligibility rules
Who can see an offer

Each offer declares an eligibility rule — a segment or PQL expression evaluated against the real-time profile. If the profile does not qualify, the offer is excluded from the ranked set for that request. Eligibility is evaluated at decision time, not at journey entry — a profile's eligibility can change between journey entry and the message send node.

Priority scoring
Which offer wins

Each eligible offer has a priority integer (0–100). Among eligible offers, the highest priority wins. AI-powered ranking (auto-optimisation) replaces the static integer with a model-predicted conversion probability per profile — requires sufficient volume (typically >10k decisions/day) to train. Below that threshold, use static priority.

Offer capping
How many times it can be shown

Offer-level capping limits total impressions (across all channels) or per-profile impressions. When an offer reaches its cap it is excluded from the ranked set regardless of priority. Capping resets on a configurable schedule. Cap reset timing must be coordinated with campaign flight dates.

Fallback offer
What serves when nothing qualifies

Every placement must define a fallback offer — a non-personalised default that serves when no eligible offer remains after capping and eligibility filtering. A placement without a fallback will produce an empty content slot in the channel template. That is a customer-facing defect, not an acceptable state.

Journey branching integration: Decision Management runs inside a journey message node — the journey does not branch on the offer outcome, it requests a decision and renders whatever the engine returns. If branching on offer outcome is required (e.g., "if profile received premium offer, send follow-up in 3 days; otherwise, send standard follow-up"), use an AEP segment that is updated post-decision as the branch condition. Direct offer-outcome branching within the same journey is not natively supported.

Agency Tip

DEPT® field pattern — maintain a "journey dependency map": a spreadsheet linking each live journey to its entry event XDM fields, AEM content fragments used, channel credentials, and frequency cap rules. Update it as a required step in every journey deployment PR. Stale maps produce orphaned journeys that consume licence quota without serving users.

Testing and preview · journey dry-run, test profile creation, channel preview

AJO testing is a multi-stage discipline. "It looks right in the designer" is not a test. A journey is ready for production when it has passed all four stages below.

  • Stage 1 · Test profile creation — AEP profiles flagged as testProfile: true are the only profiles that can enter a journey in test mode. Create test profiles that cover: entry condition satisfied, entry condition not satisfied, each major branch outcome, each suppression list scenario, and the frequency-capped state. Minimum: one profile per branch of every decision node in the journey.
  • Stage 2 · Journey dry-run (test mode) — activate the journey in test mode. Test profiles enter and traverse the journey in accelerated time (wait nodes can be collapsed to seconds). Validate: entry event fires correctly, branching evaluates as expected, each channel message is dispatched to the test profile, suppression rules prevent messages where expected, frequency caps apply. AJO's test mode logs every node evaluation — read the logs, not just the outcome.
  • Stage 3 · Channel preview — each message node has a preview function. Render the message using a test profile's attribute values and verify: personalisation tokens resolve correctly (no raw {{xdm.field}} showing in the rendered output), AEM content fragments load from the correct offer, images render, CTA URLs are correct. Preview across device types for email (desktop/mobile client rendering matrix) and push (iOS/Android OS version matrix).
  • Stage 4 · Staging sandbox end-to-end — fire a real XDM event (via Web SDK or Postman) against the staging AEP environment using a test profile identity. Verify the event appears in the profile timeline, the journey triggers, each node executes in order, and the channel message is delivered to the test device/inbox. This is the only test that validates the full data pipeline — from event emission to channel delivery.

Common Pitfall

Using Campaign Standard for a new implementation. Adobe has announced Campaign Standard's migration path to Campaign v8 (web UI). New implementations should target Campaign v8 directly to avoid a mandatory migration within 24 months of launch — a migration that will require re-implementing all workflows, all typology rules, and all integrations.

2 · Delivery architecture — IP pool governance, sending domain, SPF/DKIM/DMARC

The sending infrastructure is a decision made once and changed with significant operational cost. Get it right before the first production send.

  • IP pool segmentation — never share a single IP across transactional and marketing traffic. Transactional (order confirmations, password resets) must be isolated from batch marketing. A suppressed marketing IP must never cause a transactional failure. Minimum architecture: one transactional pool, one marketing pool. Larger programmes add per-brand and per-region pools.
  • Sending domain — use a subdomain dedicated to Campaign, never the root corporate domain. send.brand.com or email.brand.com — never brand.com. If a Campaign subdomain gets blacklisted, it does not contaminate the root domain's web reputation.
  • SPF — publish a TXT record authorising Campaign's MTA IP ranges. Include Adobe's sending infrastructure in the SPF record. SPF alone is insufficient — ISPs now weight DKIM and DMARC more heavily.
  • DKIM — Campaign signs every outbound message with a 2048-bit DKIM key on the sending subdomain. Key rotation on a 12-month schedule. DKIM alignment with the From header domain is required for DMARC pass.
  • DMARC — start with p=none and aggregate reporting enabled. Move to p=quarantine after 60 days of clean reports, then p=reject once all legitimate sending sources are confirmed. DMARC reporting endpoints should feed a monitoring dashboard — not a mailbox nobody reads.

3 · IP warming strategy — schedule, volume ramp, monitoring metrics

A new IP address has no sending reputation. ISPs apply aggressive throttling and filtering to unknown IPs. The warming schedule builds reputation by demonstrating consistent, engaged sending behaviour over 4–8 weeks before full volume is attempted.

Why This Matters

Deliverability is an architecture concern, not a marketing operations concern. A Campaign instance with no IP warming that sends 2 million emails on day one will be blacklisted by major ISPs within 48 hours. Recovery takes 4–8 weeks of restricted sending — a business stoppage event. The IP warming schedule must be in the project plan before any sending infrastructure is provisioned.

4 · Segmentation and targeting — workflow-based vs AEP audience activation

Campaign supports two distinct targeting paradigms. Choosing the wrong one for a use case produces either an operationally brittle workflow or an audience latency problem.

Campaign-native
Workflow-based targeting

AEP-driven
AEP Audience Activation

Segments built in AEP Segment Builder, activated to Campaign via the AEP Destination connector. AEP is the system of record for the unified profile. Required when targeting depends on web behaviour, offline transactions, or third-party data that lives in AEP but not in Campaign. Hybrid
AEP audience + Campaign refinement

AEP activates a broad segment; Campaign applies final exclusions (recent purchasers, active suppression, frequency-capped recipients) via workflow logic before delivery. The standard DEPT® pattern for enterprise deployments where profile data is split across both systems.

5 · Frequency capping and fatigue management — typology rules, pressure rules, control groups

Typology rules are Campaign's enforcement layer for delivery governance. They run before every delivery — not as a configuration preference but as a blocking gate. A delivery that fails a typology rule does not send.

  • Typology rule types — filtering rules (exclude specific populations), pressure rules (cap contact frequency), capacity rules (limit throughput on a channel or IP), and control rules (enforce structural requirements on the delivery itself).
  • Pressure rules — define the maximum number of contacts per recipient per sliding time window. Example: no more than 3 emails in any 7-day window, no more than 1 SMS in any 3-day window. Pressure rules evaluate across all deliveries sharing the same typology, not per-campaign — this requires a single typology scheme applied consistently across all delivery templates.
  • Control groups — extract a holdout population before delivery to measure true causal lift. Campaign's control group feature operates at the delivery level. For programme-level holdouts (withheld from all sends in a period), manage the exclusion via a dedicated Campaign schema table and a filtering typology rule.
  • Fatigue scoring — for programmes with high send frequency, implement a fatigue score in the recipient schema updated by a nightly workflow: increment on send, decrement on engagement event (open, click), and apply a pressure rule threshold against the score. Adobe Campaign integration with AEP allows this score to be shared as a profile attribute for cross-channel suppression.
Agency Tip

DEPT® field pattern — configure Campaign's typology rules in Sprint 1 as a delivery prerequisite, not a post-launch configuration. Three non-negotiable rules for every DEPT® Campaign implementation: maximum 3 emails per recipient per week, mandatory seed address list inclusion on every delivery, and suppression list refresh within 24 hours of any unsubscribe event.

6 · AEM-to-Campaign content integration — content fragment API, dynamic content blocks, personalisation tokens

AEM and Campaign integrate at the content layer via two mechanisms: the native AEM-Campaign integration (push-based, author-driven) and the Content Fragment API (pull-based, workflow-driven). They serve different authoring models and should not be conflated.

  • Native AEM-Campaign integration — authors create email templates in AEM Sites using Campaign-compatible components. The template is pushed to Campaign on activation. AEM manages authoring; Campaign manages delivery. Best suited when email templates follow page-like authoring patterns and the creative team works primarily in AEM.
  • Content Fragment API — Campaign workflows fetch fragment content via the AEM Assets HTTP API at delivery execution time. Fragments contain structured content (headline, body copy, CTA, image reference) that Campaign injects into a delivery template. Decouples content authoring lifecycle from delivery scheduling — a fragment can be updated in AEM without touching the Campaign workflow.
  • Dynamic content blocks — Campaign's native dynamic content uses JavaScript conditions against recipient attributes to select content blocks at render time. For content managed in AEM, the block content is fetched via API; the condition logic stays in Campaign. Keep condition logic in Campaign, content in AEM — do not embed business logic in AEM fragments.
  • Personalisation tokens — Campaign's <%= recipient.firstName %> syntax operates at MTA render time, after content is assembled. AEM content fragments must leave personalisation token placeholders intact — never resolve them during fragment authoring. Token validation (verifying the referenced schema field exists and is populated) must be part of the delivery pre-flight checklist.
  • High-volume pattern — for >500K sends with heavy personalisation, pre-render content variants as static HTML blocks stored in Campaign and refresh them on a schedule. Runtime API calls to AEM from the MTA at send time introduce latency and a critical-path external dependency — avoid it at scale.

7 · Bounce and unsubscribe handling — hard/soft bounce classification, suppression automation, CAN-SPAM/GDPR

Bounce and unsubscribe handling is not an operational task — it is a compliance and deliverability architecture requirement. Misclassified bounces and delayed suppressions directly damage sending reputation and expose the organisation to regulatory liability.

Hard bounce
Permanent delivery failure

SMTP 5xx codes: address does not exist, domain does not exist, address permanently rejected. Campaign must quarantine the address immediately on first hard bounce. No retry. Quarantined addresses are excluded by Campaign's built-in filtering typology — verify this rule is active on all delivery typologies.

Soft bounce
Transient delivery failure

SMTP 4xx codes: mailbox full, server temporarily unavailable, message deferred. Campaign retries on a configurable schedule (default: up to 5 days, then quarantine). Configure retry intervals to match ISP-specific retry windows — Gmail and Outlook have different expectations. After the retry period, soft bounces that never deliver are promoted to hard bounce and quarantined.

Unsubscribe
Opt-out enforcement

CAN-SPAM requires opt-out processing within 10 business days. GDPR requires it without undue delay — in practice, 72 hours is the defensible maximum. Campaign's unsubscribe workflows must write to the opt-out flag in the recipient schema and sync that flag to any connected CRM, AEP profile, or consent management platform within 24 hours. List-unsubscribe headers (RFC 8058 one-click) are now required by Gmail and Yahoo for bulk senders — configure them in Campaign's delivery template settings.

  • Suppression list architecture — maintain a master suppression list in Campaign that is the union of: hard bounces, unsubscribes, spam complaints (fed from feedback loops with Gmail, Yahoo, Outlook), and any legal suppression orders. This list must be the first exclusion applied in every targeting workflow, before any segment logic runs.
  • Feedback loop registration — register with ISP feedback loop programmes (Gmail Postmaster Tools, Yahoo Complaint Feedback Loop, Microsoft SNDS) to receive spam complaint data. These feeds must flow into Campaign's suppression list automatically — a manual process will always lag.
  • GDPR right to erasure — a Campaign deletion workflow triggered by a data subject request must delete or anonymise the recipient record, hard-delete all delivery logs referencing that recipient, and write confirmation to the request tracking system. Do not implement this as a manual operation.

8 · Campaign MID-sourcing architecture — when required, how it changes delivery routing

MID-sourcing (Message Centre or Marketing/Mid routing) is a Campaign topology where the marketing instance delegates actual SMTP delivery to one or more dedicated mid-sourcing servers. It is not the default topology — it is required for specific scenarios and changes the delivery routing model materially.

  • When MID-sourcing is required — when the marketing instance is in a network zone without direct outbound SMTP access to the internet (common in highly regulated environments); when throughput requirements exceed what a single Campaign application server MTA can deliver; when delivery must be isolated from the main Campaign application for security or compliance segmentation; and in multi-region deployments where a regional MID server reduces SMTP latency to local ISPs.
  • Routing change — in a standard topology, Campaign's internal MTA delivers directly to ISPs. With MID-sourcing, the marketing instance sends delivery jobs (message payloads + recipient lists) to the mid-sourcing instance over an internal API. The mid instance owns the MTA, the IP pool, and all SMTP sessions with ISPs. The marketing instance receives delivery status callbacks from the mid instance and updates its tracking logs. The marketing instance has no direct connection to ISPs.
  • Operational implication — deliverability monitoring, bounce processing, and IP reputation management now occur on the mid-sourcing instance, not the marketing instance. Ensure DKIM keys, SPF records, and feedback loop registrations are associated with the mid-sourcing server's IP ranges and sending domains — not the marketing application server's.
  • Hybrid MID patterns — some programmes use MID-sourcing for bulk marketing sends and retain direct MTA on the marketing instance for low-volume transactional sends. This requires two delivery routing configurations in Campaign and two distinct IP pools — one per path. Typology rules must enforce which delivery type uses which routing.

2 · Inference cost architecture

Every LLM API call has a cost measured in tokens. At low traffic, cost is invisible. At scale, it is a product budget line. Inference cost architecture is the discipline of designing LLM usage so that cost scales sublinearly with traffic — not linearly, not superlinearly.

  • Call budgeting — define a per-user, per-session, and per-feature monthly token budget before shipping. Treat it like a database query budget. Uncapped LLM calls in production is a runaway cost incident waiting to happen.
  • Model selection — frontier models (GPT-4o, Claude Opus, Gemini Ultra) are 10–30× more expensive per token than mid-tier models (GPT-4o-mini, Claude Haiku, Gemini Flash). Use frontier models only where reasoning complexity demands it; default to mid-tier for classification, summarisation, and structured extraction.
  • Fine-tuned models — a fine-tuned mid-tier model on domain-specific tasks can match frontier model quality at mid-tier cost. The break-even point is roughly 50K training examples and sustained high-volume inference. Worth the investment for verticalised products.
  • Batch vs streaming — streaming (token-by-token) is required for conversational UX. Batch processing (async, no streaming) costs 50% less on Anthropic and OpenAI batch APIs. For non-real-time workloads — nightly content enrichment, bulk classification, report generation — always use batch.

3 · Prompt caching patterns

Prompt caching allows LLM providers to reuse the KV-cache of a previous computation when the prefix of the prompt is identical. Anthropic charges 10% of the normal input token price for cache hits; OpenAI charges 50%. For applications with a large, stable system prompt or static context, this is the single highest-leverage cost reduction available — and it must be designed in at the prompt architecture stage, not bolted on later.

Cache this
System prompt

Role definition, instructions, tone, format rules, tool descriptions. Identical across all users. Typically 80% of total input tokens. Cache hit rate targets: above 85%.

Cache this
Static context

Product documentation, knowledge base articles, long reference documents passed as context. Changes infrequently. Cache with a long TTL; invalidate on content update.

Do not cache
Dynamic user context

User name, account state, current session history, real-time personalisation data. Unique per user per session. Caching it would either poison the cache or expose data across users.

4 · Multi-model orchestration

No single model is optimal for all tasks on cost, latency, and quality simultaneously. Multi-model orchestration routes requests to the right model based on declared signals, with fallback chains for availability and cost protection.

Routing
Complexity-based routing

A lightweight classifier (fine-tuned or rules-based) scores incoming requests as simple, moderate, or complex. Simple queries go to cheap models; complex reasoning goes to frontier. The classifier itself must cost near-zero.

Routing
Cost-based routing

Real-time token budget monitoring per feature, user, or session. When the budget ceiling is approached, route to cheaper models or cached responses. Prevent runaway spend without degrading the majority of traffic.

Routing
Latency-based routing

For time-sensitive UX paths (sub-500ms user-facing response), route to the fastest available model regardless of cost. For background jobs, route to batch or slower models. Latency SLA is set per feature, not per platform.

Resilience
Fallback chains

Primary → secondary model → cached response → non-LLM baseline. Provider outages are not hypothetical. A platform with no fallback chain has LLM availability as a single point of failure for any feature that depends on it.

5 · LLM-powered personalisation

Personalisation via LLM falls into two delivery patterns: real-time assembly (generate per-user response at request time) and pre-generated variant caching (generate N variants at publish time, serve the best match). The right choice is determined by user population size, privacy constraints, and cost tolerance.

  • Real-time assembly — high fidelity, high cost. Use when the personalisation space is too large for pre-generation (individual user state, real-time signals). Requires latency budget and prompt caching for the static layers.
  • Pre-generated variant caching — low cost, limited fidelity. Generate M variants for N persona segments at content-publish time, cache aggressively, serve from edge CDN. Zero inference cost at request time. Suitable for content personalisation at audience-segment level.
  • Privacy constraints — user context passed to external LLM APIs is data processing under GDPR. The minimum viable data principle applies: pass only what is necessary for the specific personalisation task, never full profiles. Pseudonymisation before API calls is the architectural default.
Common Pitfall

Passing full user profiles — email, name, full browsing history — to external LLM APIs for personalisation without a data processing agreement with the LLM provider. This is a GDPR breach under Article 28 (data processor requirements), not a technical debt item. Review what user context enters any external LLM API call before the feature ships.

6 · LLM latency SLAs

LLM inference is inherently slower than deterministic computation. Treating it as a drop-in replacement for a database query in a synchronous request path produces UX failure. Latency SLAs for LLM features must be agreed, measured, and enforced with the same rigour as API SLAs.

Target
P50 · median

Time-to-first-token under 400ms for user-facing streaming. Fully complete response under 2s for short outputs. The experience the majority of users have — optimise this first.

Target
P95 · tail

Time-to-first-token under 1.5s. Complete response under 8s. Where provider congestion and long prompts compound. Alert on P95 breaches before they become P50 problems.

Target
P99 · worst case

Hard timeout at 15–20s with graceful degradation to non-LLM baseline. Never let a slow LLM response block the entire page render. Timeout handling is not optional.

Pattern
Graceful degradation

Every LLM feature has a non-LLM fallback: static content, cached response, or rule-based alternative. The degraded state must be acceptable UX, not an error page. Design the fallback before designing the LLM path.

7 · LLMO content audit

Before investing in new content production, audit what exists for LLM extractability. Most established sites have substantial content that is already accurate and authoritative — but structured for human reading and keyword ranking, not for LLM citation. The audit converts existing content value into LLMO value at lower cost than new production.

  • Answer-readiness score — does each page answer a specific question in the first 50 words? Score pages 0–3. Pages scoring 0 are candidates for structural rewrite (opening paragraph only, not full rewrite).
  • Schema coverage — which pages have JSON-LD structured data? Which schema types are present? FAQ and Article schema have the highest citation impact per implementation hour.
  • Citation attribution — does each piece of content carry a named author, publication date, and last-reviewed date? Anonymous, undated content is deprioritised by LLM citation systems.
  • LLM render test — fetch the page as a plain-text HTTP request (no JS execution). What does an LLM crawler see? If the answer is nothing, the content is invisible to LLM-based indexing regardless of its quality.
  • llms.txt presence — does the domain root have an llms.txt? If not, LLM crawlers are operating without declared intent boundaries — which can result in either over-crawling or under-citation.

The Vocabulary

Event Vocabulary

When AI is wiring components together, the names of the events become the most-used surface in the codebase. The architect's job is to own that vocabulary like a product surface — versioned, documented, governed.

What architects govern

  • Names — the domain:action namespace. cart:add, user:login, search:filterChanged. No change, no update, no untyped strings.
  • Payload contracts — the shape of each event's detail. Required fields, optional fields, value types.
  • Producers and consumers — who emits each event, who is allowed to listen. Pub/sub is decoupled, but ownership is not.
  • Versioning — when an event's payload changes shape, what happens to existing listeners. Breaking-change policy applies.

The event registry — one place

An event vocabulary that lives in everyone's head doesn't survive a team change. Architects maintain a single registry — a markdown file, a wiki page, a typed module — that lists every event, its payload, its producer, its consumers, and its status (active, deprecated, removed). The AI prompt references this registry; new features add to it before they ship.

Architect's Review · Event Vocabulary
  • Where is the event registry, and who owns it?
  • Does every event follow the domain:action convention?
  • Are payload contracts documented alongside event names?
  • What is the deprecation policy for retired events?
  • Does the Stalwart prompt reference the registry?
30-second scan
  • What you will learn: how the four contract types — API, Event, Data, and Component — combine into a single contract-driven development workflow where architects define, contracts are versioned, AI generates, engineers review against the contract, and CI validates conformance
  • Why it matters: contract-driven development is the model that makes large-scale AI code generation safe — without it every AI-generated PR is a full-code review with no starting frame of reference
  • Contract versioning is not optional — a contract that changes without a version bump is a breaking change that cannot be tracked
  • Consumer-driven contract testing (Pact) extends the model to API consumers, making it possible to validate that a change does not break any downstream consumer before deployment
Why This Matters

Contracts reduce review time by changing the review question. Without a contract, the reviewer asks "is this correct?" — an unbounded question requiring full context reconstruction. With a contract, the reviewer asks "does this satisfy the contract?" — a bounded question answerable by comparison. The contract is what makes review scalable across a large AI-augmented team.

Contract-Driven Development

What this changes

Code review shifts. Reviewers no longer ask "is this the line I would have written?" — they ask "does this implementation honour the contract?" That's a faster, more objective, more delegable review. It also means architects spend their time where their leverage is — drafting and versioning contracts — instead of in PRs.

The four contracts (from Module 10) all participate:

  • API contracts drive api.js generation in Stalwart.
  • Event contracts drive biz.js and events.js wiring.
  • Data contracts drive model.js schemas and validations.
  • Component contracts drive reusable UI generation.
Code is generated from contracts. Reviews check conformance to contracts. Bugs trace back to contract gaps. Architecture becomes contract management.

  • Are contracts in version control alongside code?
  • Is there a contract for every external surface (API, event, data, component)?

  • What you will learn: The split between what AI generates and what humans govern — three human-owned domains (contracts, review, deployment) and the governance mechanisms that make agentic development safe at DEPT® scale
  • Why it matters: AI governance is not a policy document — it is an architecture — the mechanisms (contract validation, review gates, deployment controls) must be implemented in CI before any AI-generated code reaches production
  • The three human-owned domains are the irreducible minimum of human accountability in an AI-augmented engineering team — they are not negotiable trade-offs
  • Governance debt accumulates faster than technical debt in AI-generated codebases — each un-reviewed PR is a precedent that weakens the governance model
Why This Matters

Human ownership of deployment is non-negotiable. An AI agent with deployment permissions acting on a misunderstood brief can take a production system offline. The deployment trigger must always require a human decision — not because AI is unreliable but because accountability requires a decision-maker who can be held responsible and who can exercise judgement in ambiguous situations.

Common Pitfall

Governance frameworks that exist at the policy level but not at the CI level. The policy says "all AI-generated code must be reviewed by a senior engineer." The CI check does not exist. Result: policy is aspirational; junior engineers merge AI-generated code under deadline pressure. CI is governance; policy is intent. Without CI, you have only intent.

The Governance

AI Governance — the new role split

In an AI-assisted team the work doesn't disappear, but it moves. The architect's job becomes more about what AI is allowed to produce and less about writing it themselves. Drawing the line clearly is the governance.

AI generates

Components · Functions · Tests · Data mappings · API clients · Boilerplate · Documentation drafts · Migration scripts

Humans govern

Architecture · Security model · Contracts · Event vocabulary · Release strategy · Prompt templates · Failure modes · What the product should be

Three things only humans should own

  • What gets built — product intent, business priorities, the "why."
  • The shape it must take — contracts, file rosters, event vocabulary, architectural rules.
  • What it means to be safe — security, compliance, audit, the things you cannot ship if wrong.

Everything else can flow through the AI loop. The architect who tries to govern everything ends up governing nothing. The architect who governs these three sets the whole system's quality ceiling.

Architect's Review · AI Governance
  • Is the AI-vs-human split written down for this team?
  • Who approves changes to prompt templates?
  • Who approves changes to event vocabulary and contracts?
  • Is there a clear path for "AI got it wrong" — escalation and rollback?
  • How is AI-generated code attributed, and is that auditable?
AI Routing

When to Use AI — and When Not To

The most expensive mistake in AI-native development isn't a bad prompt — it's routing the wrong task to an LLM at all. Token cost is the obvious waste; the hidden cost is the latency, context-switching, and cognitive overhead of waiting for an AI to do something a one-line shell command would solve in 200 milliseconds.

30-second scan
  • What you will learn: Which tasks should never touch an LLM, which model tier fits which task, and when to reach for a web search, a CLI, or a tool directly
  • Why it matters: Defaulting every task to the most capable model is the fastest way to burn token budget on work a grep would handle — and to train your team to wait 8 seconds for things that should be instant
  • LLMs are reasoning engines, not command runners — anything the shell or a known tool does deterministically should stay in the shell
  • Model selection is a product decision: the wrong tier in production costs real money; the wrong tier in development costs developer flow
  • Web search is not a model capability — it is a different tool with different semantics (current, factual, retrievable vs synthesised, probabilistic, trained-to)

The Don't-Route-to-AI List

These tasks have a faster, cheaper, more reliable answer that is not an LLM. If you find yourself prompting an AI for any of these, stop.

git commit, push, pull, merge, stash You know these. Deterministic CLI, no reasoning required. git directly

Common Pitfall

Prompting an AI to write a git commit message for a one-line fix. You wrote the code; you know what changed. git commit -m "fix: null check on user email" takes 4 seconds. Waiting for an AI to write that message takes 8 seconds and produces a message you'll edit anyway. Reserve AI for commit messages on complex multi-file changes where the "why" is genuinely hard to articulate.

Every additional capability tier costs tokens, latency, and money. The right model is the least capable model that can do the task reliably. Defaulting to the top model is not safe — it's expensive and slower.

Agency Tip

In Claude Code, switch models with /model. The rule DEPT® uses on active projects: start a session on Sonnet 4.6 (the default workhorse). Escalate to Sonnet 4.7 when the task involves more than 5 files or the problem is genuinely ambiguous. Escalate to Opus only after Sonnet has failed or the task is explicitly high-stakes (security, compliance, architecture). Drop to Haiku for any task where you need speed over depth — quick lookups, boilerplate generation, short summaries. Staying on the wrong tier for an hour is the most common token waste pattern on development teams.

Why This Matters

A team of 5 engineers that defaults everything to Opus 4.7 instead of routing correctly will spend 8–15× the token budget of a team with a routing discipline — for no meaningful quality difference on the 80% of tasks that Sonnet handles at parity. Token budget is finite. Routing is how you make it last.

The Decision Rule

Ask in order
1. Can the shell or a known tool do this deterministically? → Use the shell.
2. Is this a factual or current-state lookup? → Use web search.
3. Is this irreversible or production-critical? → Manual tool. Human runs it.
4. Is this a simple completion or single-file task? → Haiku or Sonnet 4.6.
5. Is this a complex multi-file or ambiguous task? → Sonnet 4.7.
6. Has Sonnet failed, or is this genuinely high-stakes? → Opus. Justify the cost.
